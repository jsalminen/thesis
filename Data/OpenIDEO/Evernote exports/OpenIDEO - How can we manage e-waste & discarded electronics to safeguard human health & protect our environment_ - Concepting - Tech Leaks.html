Tech Leaks

Platform to share cases of planned obsolescence and identified predetermined braking points.
Steph Dunn, bulb

The concept is inspired by Wiki Leaks and the documentation "The Light Bulb Conspiracy".

A web platform is used to report cases of planned obsolescence. Predetermined braking points will be public. Also solutions like the software patch for printer shown in the documentation can be shared over the platform. 


Foto: Steph Dunn

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

A higher awareness among consumers will start a change within big companies to fulfill this need.
Sharing solutions to make devices last longer will decrease the total amount of e-waste.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

Creating an awareness and transparency for consumers and starting to move companies towards long lasting products.

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

A Web-expert would be needed to set up the platform. Server capacity is needed as well.

Hi Sabine - I like your thinking here. There is definitely a need to identify planned obsolescence in electrical and electronic devices (including things like manipulation of operating systems and the other side of the coin like as you say with software patches and in James Moyer's concept ( http://www.openideo.com/open/e-waste/concepting/keeping-older-smart-devices-usable-by-encouraging-the-development-of-fast-software-for-them/ ))
Here we have a magazine and website called Choice
( http://www.choice.com.au/ ) which has a reputation for its fearless independence. I think yours is an idea that organisations like Choice would embrace.
Often manufacturers don't like unfavourable comparisons but the smarter ones at least tend to fix the identified problems in the next model. Reporting on planned obsolescence and the breaking points (planned or otherwise) might encourage companies to increase the life of products and actually enable them to introduce what the auto industry refers to as after-market enhancements.

To think about cell phones it is clear that manufacturers have the capacity to apparently quickly respond to changes in network architecture (3G to 4G for example). I reality the network architecture changes far slower than the circuit design changes needed to adapt to them and manufacturers are aware very early of directions the networks are taking. Similarly developments in some hardware like processors and touch screens occur far more slowly than the circuitry and software to take advantage of them - some of this lag time could easily be devoted to enabling existing products to embrace these changes too, even if it means swapping out some components, upgrading memory etc.

In thinking a little more about this I am reminded of the notion of "crippleware" and trial versions (like Microsoft products) that require a tweak (usually involving money) to restore full functionality. Although frustrating to the (non-paying) user it does demonstrate capacity to build in future use.
