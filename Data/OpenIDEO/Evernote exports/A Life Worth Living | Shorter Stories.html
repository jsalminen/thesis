A Life Worth Living

Posted on
April 1, 2012

“Luc, what are you doing with your life? Tell me!”

<silence>

“You are DRIFTING! And you are too good to drift. I won’t allow it. What are you *doing* with your *LIFE*?! What is your contribution going to be?!”

I love my friends. They’re so supportive. And always know just what to say! This is a very personal story which I offer as an explanation for my interest in hopes, dreams and creating a life worth living. Sharing it is also a bit of an emotional stretching exercise as I take my first tentative steps into this new blogging medium. Please be gentle

Anyway, back to me being adrift, directionless and not doing anything with my life. At the time I faced those questions (asked exactly like that, I might add) my first response was HUH?! Everything in life seemed to be in order. Great job, check. Loving long-term partner, check. Fantastic friends, check. Living on a quiet island paradise, check. By most measures of success, I was doing well – very well, even – particularly for my age. So why, then, was I so uncomfortable when confronted with this enquiry? Could it be true? Was I drifting?

Over the months that followed (and it was months – I’m dumber than I look), I considered… What is my contribution to the world going to be? What do I want my life to be about? I understood that these questions weren’t about a career plan or what job I wanted… but what impact I wanted my life (including my various careers) to have on the world at large.

All of this coincided with the Christchurch earthquakes and the lessons which attended them. Following the February quake, I flew down to check in with friends and family and help with the clean-up. Things were so much worse than they looked on TV – the smell, the torn roads, the lopsided houses – particularly out in the East. The hopes and dreams of entire communities were buried in the rubble of their lives.  And yet, in the midst of all this devastation, I was fortunate to witness the kindness, generosity and resilience of Canterbury as it came together to set things right. The visit was a lesson in the fragility of life, the strength of the human spirit and just how precious our allotted time is.

On the flight home, I daydreamed a little, gazing out the window at the sunlight bouncing off the blanket of clouds below. With a clarity and determination which I had lacked when I left Auckland, I decided that I wanted (like, really wanted) my life to mean something, to contribute something to the world… and it was with that sense of purpose that I got out one of those A4 notebooks and started writing… words, phrases, feelings, imagery… babble, really. But by the time I got home, I had it. I want to contribute to…

A world where people dare to hope, dream and (most importantly) act to be everything they can be.

I want to be a purveyor of positive change, an agency for realising potential. As a statement, this possibly won’t seem that momentous for you – I may come across as a dreamer or even arrogant. Perhaps I’m a touch of both. But for me, it was transformational to see what I do now and what I could do in future through this lens of helping people to be everything they can be. If I get to the end of my life and, looking back, I discover that is the theme, it will have been a life worth living… I will be a very happy man…

..and I will be grateful to the friend who challenged me to respect the time I’ve been given – to honour it by being clear about what I’m doing here.
