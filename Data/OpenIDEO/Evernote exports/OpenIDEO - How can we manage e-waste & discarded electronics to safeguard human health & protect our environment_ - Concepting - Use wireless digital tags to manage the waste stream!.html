Use wireless digital tags to manage the waste stream!

Apply RFID or NFC chips to our tech products. These chips let you grab information by placing them near a scanner. Use them to store useful info that could help e-waste collectors better manage the waste stream and help consumers find collectors.
RFID Chip (so small!) Public domain photo via Wikimedia Commons
Let's use wireless, digital tags to better manage and track the e-waste stream!

Background info: Wireless technologies such as Radio-Frequency Identification (RFID) and Near Field Communication (NFC) allow bits of information to be wirelessly transmitted over short distance. Digital 'tags' can store information and send it to 'readers' when they are placed near each other. The tags require no power source as they are powered by the electromagnetic field generated by the reader. Essentially they receive the request for information and use the energy from that signal to 'bounce back' the information. Walmart uses this technology to track inventory and the US military uses it to track supplies.

NFC is built upon the principles of RFID and is becoming increasingly commonplace in the smartphone industry as it allows more versatile features. Google is behind NFC which is a good sign that it will be the standard in smartphones down the road. So NFC would be a great option to get consumers involved. 

The Concept:
Embed RFID or NFC tags in electronic devices so that e-waste collectors can better manage the waste stream. A standardized set of Life Cycle Information on the tags could provide serial numbers and a Bill of Materials (a list of all materials in the product and their amounts). As storage is limited on these tags, it may be necessary to use the tags as IDs that pull information from a database. 

That same database could store a history of the device. As it moves from factory to store to e-waste facility it could 'check in.' This would require a unique ID for each device. Even if the tag only identified the model, this data could provide amazing insight into a products life cycle in the long run. (Paul Reader touched on tracking in his labeling concept)

Retailers could use the chips to manage inventory as well as track deposits if an electronics deposit program were adopted. 

These same chips could be used to help consumers determine where to recycle their gadgets. Just like Vincent Cheng's concept, but with chips instead of QR codes. Consumers with NFC readers in their phones could use a companion app to determine the best course of action!

The chips could even be embedded on individual components to track parts on machines designed for disassembly and upgrade. 

Summary of Benefits
  • Helps e-waste collectors manage and track waste stream
  • Helps e-waste recyclers determine materials inside
  • Helps retailers track inventory and deposits
  • Helps consumers find collectors
  • Based on existing standards
  • Could track the entire life cycle in a centralized location and provide meaningful insight

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

It helps the E-waste collection and processing industry manage and track waste. It could also help consumers determine where to send their gadgets.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

The integration of the chips would be part of the design phase. At the point of sale they could be used to track deposits (made to to ensure recycling). At the point of disposal they help the consumer determine where to bring it and help the collectors process and manage the waste stream.

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

Create a standard format for the information to be stored on the tags.
Create a universal symbol to mark where the tags are hiding
Create partnerships

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

I think the most important resource will be a partnership with e-waste collectors, distributers and recyclers.

My only suggestion here is to flesh out the "track waste stream" particulars. You could even have the detection of e-waste be done at the curbside, before the trash is even collected.

Moreover, passive RFIDs are so cheap now that they can be attached to anything--even single disposable batteries.

I like your innovation in how the RFID would contain information about the product to help recycling.
