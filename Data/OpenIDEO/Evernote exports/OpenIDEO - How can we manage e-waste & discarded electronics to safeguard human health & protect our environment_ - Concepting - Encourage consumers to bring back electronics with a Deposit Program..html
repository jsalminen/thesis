Encourage consumers to bring back electronics with a Deposit Program.

Apply an old school method of ensuring beverage container reuse/recycling to consumer electronics. Have consumers pay an extra deposit when purchasing electronics that they will be refunded for recycling.
Return for Refund taken by nerissa's ring on Flickr
For as long as I can remember, my family back home has had milk delivered. It has always in a glass bottle. When the bottles are empty, you put them in the cooler on the porch. When new milk is delivered, the empties are taken. For each bottle they give you, they charge a small deposit (less than a dollar). For each bottle you return they refund you that amount (as credit on your bill). 

Why not adopt this simple model that's been around for decades for consumer electronics?

When you go to buy a new smartphone, you pay an extra fee around, let's say $20. When you come back down the road to buy a new phone, you return that old one any condition (no matter how fried, zapped or smashed!) and get that money back. You can now use your old deposit to cover the new one. If you buy a new phone without returning the old one, now you have to pay another depot, penalizing you for not properly recycling. 

The fees would clearly depend on the type of electronic. Batteries and alarm clocks would be a lot lower then that shiny new laptop you've been eyeing. 

Once collected, the electronics would be sent to a proper sorting facility. Usable items would be refurbished and sold for reuse. Others would be recycled in an effective manner. No shipping oversees to be smashed and burned in someone's garage. The deposit money could theoretically help invest in new recycling facilities, and the profits earned could then help pay back the deposits. 

This model could be adopted on by either the manufacturers, the retailers or a government. Preferably some sort of collaboration. 

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

By encouraging proper recycling (or reuse) we are conserving resources. The more metals we can salvage the less we need to mine for. By making sure people remove old electronics from their home, we potentially remove toxins that can leech out and cause harm. And by ensuring that the recycling is done responsibly, we protect exploited workers in the developing works from mishandling toxic substances.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

This model keeps electronics out of the landfills and out of our attics. It encourages consumers to bring back their used electronics with a monetary incentive (one of the best kinds of incentives!)

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

Negotiations with large consumer electronics stores would be an important stepping stone. They are critical to the plan. They would be accepting and refunding the deposits as well as collecting the old goods.

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

Partnerships with major retailers - both on and offline (such as Best Buy and Newegg.com)
Government backing of the program (could help speed things along)
Partnerships with responsible electronics recyclers

Andrew Allen

July 17, 2012, 09:36PM
Great concept! This idea can certainly become as "everyday" as deposits are now in some states. Since reading your concept, I've been thinking about this idea and I definitely want to see it happen sometime soon. I'm not sure how deep into the details we want to get in the concepting phase but here are some of my thoughts:

The challenge will be with the implementation, specifically with the retailers. (Using phones as an example, consumers might be turned off by inconsistencies among retailers. They also won't initially understand the idea that they'll only pay once for all the smartphones they'll ever own, and instead they only see the additional price.) However, much of the problem could be solved, as has been mentioned, if this is introduced as a law (as opposed to a program one company starts and others adopt).

Some details remaining to be ironed out would deal with the return of the deposit: Using the soda can deposits model as an example, in Oregon (and I'm assuming elsewhere) certain brands of soda cans (usually off-brand) need to be taken to specific grocery stores in order to get the deposit back. Translated to this electronics deposit idea, what happens when I purchase a more long-term electronic device that's not so quickly phased out like smartphones (such as a stereo receiver)...and years later I can't remember where I purchased it? Where do I get the deposit? This problem could be solved by the law, but as experience with the soda deposit law in Oregon shows, not all cans are accepted everywhere, even with a law.

Anyway, I love this idea. The above comments are not criticisms but rather additional (and admittedly nit-picky) details to consider as you further develop this promising concept. Good luck!
