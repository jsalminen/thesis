Recy-COOL-ability (Update 1)

A brand, a campaign, a logo, a label, a database and a web site for awareness, education and practical information for e-waste control.

Awareness - the Vital Ingredient

  • Bringing the consumer's attention to the benefits and advantages of products that have been and can be repaired, reused or recycled goes a long way towards changing attitudes and behaviours;
  • Involving children both as young consumers of some products and future users of others builds future behaviours and attitudes;
  • Giving appropriate products a mark of distinction sets them apart and gives them a competitive edge; and
  • Building acceptance of a brand representing desirable quality regardless of who has actually manufactured them brings that quality to the fore.

Mira Rao's concept on Marketing Campaigns sets out some directions and examples for building awareness across this and many other concepts in this challenge: As does Valentina's Schools Marketing Campaign concept.

Core of the Concept

The core of this concept is a repairability/recyclability/reusability/reduction rating which is applied to all new electrical and electronic products. It is analogous in many ways to the Energy Star concept and indeed is complementary to it.

Most electrical and electronic appliances now have either an Energy Star label (provided the product meets the criteria established by the EPA) or an energy rating label where the number of stars represents the level of energy efficiency.
The recy-cool-able concept rates electrical and electronic appliances by their repairability (and/or upgradeability, reusability), recyclability, and environmental friendliness (reduction).

There will be a labeling regime similar to the energy star system but with the following differences:

  • Instead of stars for energy efficiency the label has penguins;
  • It incorporates the recognisable Reduce/Reuse/Recycle arrow system slightly modified; and
  • Where the energy rating is labelled by the more stars the better the recy-cool-able label reflects the criteria by the completeness of colour in each penguin's body.


Campaign / Logos

Because the labels would be applied to products that comply (albeit to different extents) the label becomes a visible, recognisable link between product, consumer and recycling. Elements of the design become logos that can be adopted for other purposes such as advertising of recyclers, mapping and education (discussed a little more below).


Why recy-cool-able?

Just a made-up word that I hope implies that it is 'cool' to recycle e-waste, provides a 'cool' link to the penguins and a unique URL (recycoolable.org - already registered).


The Penguins

Each penguin represents an important criteria in the journey from manufacture to final recycling.

There are 3 penguins green, blue and yellow.

  • Green - represents eco-friendliness - so the greener that penguin's body becomes the less toxic are the materials it contains;

  • Blue - represents recyclability so the bluer that penguin's body becomes the more easily the item can be recycled; and

  • Yellow - represents repairability so the yellower that penguins body becomes the more easily it can be repaired or upgraded.

The colour of each penguin begins at approx. 1% just to identify which is which then progresses in 6 banded steps between 1% and 100% - so each one becomes similar to the 1-6 star system for energy rating and green building ratings. I think there are sound reasons behind the six-star system so I have followed that convention.

In addition to the penguins, larger labels include a QR code for detailed product information and a 'number' representing the percentage of recycled material used in the product manufacture.

The actual approach to representation is open to variation and examples of smaller labels for smaller products will be included in further diagrams.

The concepts from Robert Caruso  ( Deposits and Upgrading ) and
 Arash Shoushtari ( Leasing Model ) lead in the direction of exchanging  devices in whole or in part.
This might require an alternative label (see additional and alternative labels diagrams) representing a device that can perhaps be either exchanged completely or that can be disassembled by the end user and have components exchanged.

 
Why 'penguins'?

Penguins tend to have a fairly universal appeal as representative of eco-friendliness, second only to pandas.
They are easily (for a better artist than me) represented stylistically and the usual black and white body lends itself to colour coding.
Although the energy star has some appeal to children it is primarily a mechanism for adult identification, whereas the penguins become identifiable to the 'whole family'.

A further aspect of this concept I shall come to a little later is that using penguins can lead to educational outcomes and advertising appeal across all age groups.

What the banding means.

The colouring of the penguin bodies needs to have some technical meaning too and these would be determined by criteria suggested here by the OpenIDEO  community but finally under the control of the EPA.

Broadly though, just as a 3.5 star refrigerator might represent good energy efficiency (say 384 kwh per year - see fridge energy ratings), so a 3 green band penguin (60% green) would indicate fewer toxic elements and compounds included in manufacture than a single green band penguin. Given there are upwards of fifteen toxic elements or compounds commonly found in electronic devices a formula could be devised for gauging the number and amount of each contributing to a toxicity index that would then translate into green banding which increases with eco-friendliness of the product.

Similarly blue banding might be predicated on modularity of construction, ease of component exchange and other factors leading to a repairability index.

Finally yellow banding might be dependent on ease of dismantling, actual recyclability or reusability of components, and perhaps whether the components are suitable for localized recycling compared to centralised.

The system could be modified to accommodate further colours for other desirable criteria although I think three is probably optimum.

The importance and detail of the criteria would tend to vary between products just as the energy rating varies. On the other hand the visual cue would have the same 'more is better' meaning to the purchaser.

Why no red penguin?
Given that red is often used to indicated danger (although yellow and black stripes have similar warning properties) I thought having a red penguin might have a negative effect. Additionally there are some product coding systems that use traffic light (red, amber, green) coding for other purposes. However this is a concept - not a fixed solution.

Label Design

In the diagram I have interposed the penguins between each Reduce/Reuse/Recycle  arrow with the green (eco-friendly) penguin at the apex and the others at the bottom corners.

Again, although I am somewhat wedded to the penguin idea, it is the colour-coding and signalling principle that should be emphasised.

Additional and alternative designs will be included in additional diagrams that can be used for different purposes as described in other parts of this concept..


Testing Reporting and Record Keeping

While the colour banding on the label is a visual clue to the consumer indicating that (in terms of recycling) one appliance or product is superior, the detail underlying this becomes useful for the repair, re-use and recycling industry (much as the numbering of plastics indicate their composition).
Part of the process in awarding label ratings would be availability of detailed information on toxicity, substrate and casing materials, circuitry information, and replacement part availability and/or design specifications
(see Designed for Dis-assembly and Dis-assembling laser printer )

Standards Testing
Appliances and products seeking energy rating are subject to rigorous testing already. To some extent where physical inspection of appliances is required this could be incorporated as part of existing product acceptance testing. In other instances such as toxicity the requirements could become similar to existing materials (or product) safety data sheets.

For repairability the past common practice of including circuit diagrams or schematics with the product would be replaced by online availability.
Ease of dismantling could be established as part of physical inspection, including such things as availability of specialised dismantling tools.

Virtually all of the required information is already available through the design and manufacturing of these products so making it available to form the basis of a rating system would not be too onerous for manufacturers.

The EPA already has a comprehensive product database for the energy star system, requiring the manufacturers to supply the verifiable data in a format suitable for inclusion in EPA's existing system would require additional storage overhead and some additional programming but nothing compared to setting up from scratch.
It would probably be appropriate for the EPA database to just point to some manufacturer's data sheets.(e.g.  an expanded version of Apple Product Environmental Reports).
Furthermore, to the extent that this is not already done, such a system would be shared amongst countries adopting the standards providing complementary verification.

Greg's suggestion for Tracking Bulk Flow could simplify the process and possibly make it viable (compared to getting to the product level for materials).

Funding

Core charges together with a universally agreed pricing penalty applied to differentially compliant products. That is to say, each manufacturer contributes a base value for the cost of compliance in the landed cost of the product together with an additional variable component cost based on established recy-cool-ability (the more compliant the less penalty).

Alternatively just a simple fee based on the type of product.

The system embodies the Reduce/Reuse/Recycle principle since products are awarded value for reduced toxicity, and increased reusability and recyclability.

Advertising

Like the energy star system it is self-promoting, being a desirable award for environmental friendliness and recyclability. It can therefore be included not only in product labelling but also in advertising as a recognisable selling point or point of difference between products.

Recyclers can utilise components of the label as an indication that they accept such products as a premium input. This could be integrated with  Fabio Calzavara's Recycling Network.

Education
(see also website)

Alluding again to the general universal appeal of penguins it becomes a useful tool to teach children, and through them their parents, the value of considering not only the acquisition and use of products but their eventual disposal as well.

Incorporating penguins can lead to colouring competitions and other ways to engage younger children
e.g. National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences:Kids Pages

Older children can be taught a little more of the concept behind the symbols
e.g. Educate consumers when they are young

Teachers can have access to greater detail which might lead to choice of suitable products for dismantling to explore the greater meaning in the Reduce/Reuse/Recycle concept e.g. for Learn by (Un)doing .

Project materials can be developed around a consistent visual theme.

R2 Compliance

The system lends itself to providing R2 compliance (Responsible Recycling) from the design stage onwards.
In addition to informing the consumer it also informs the recycling industry through the underlying data structure and information.
Recyclers can be better informed about products which hopefully will reach them in better general condition as the public becomes more aware of the recycling potential in each product. If the labeling is persistent and backed by readily available data products can be identified sufficiently well as they enter the recycling stream to be directed towards the most appropriate method - e.g. appliances of known low toxicity and ease of dismantling can be subject to simpler recycling processes and will attract a higher premium on materials recovered.

QR Code
(possibly replaced by Andrew Allen's E-Rating system QR code or the RRR QR Code where appropriate)

Linking the label to the specific product data.
This was inspired directly by Make Recycling Easier wherever it is done from Emily Bartlett and Product Labeling from Fabio Calzavara .
This could contain either a simple reference to the product data sheets or information available from the manufacturer, and or from the EPA. It could also contain actual data using material coding, percentages etc.

As with most other aspects of this concept the detail would be that determined most desirable by the EPA.

With Vincent Cheng's Realtime Recycling Recs (QR Code) concept the code from this (recycoolable) label could be substituted by the RRR QR Code for a range of (or even all) products thereby possibly extending their life. If the product is cross-referenced to the materials database it could achieve both objectives.


Website

Notionally (recycoolable.org) but prototyped on free hosting.
This would be for the brand, campaign, information about the concept and links to education on e-waste.
It would not be the repository for product specific data - that is between the manufacturers and the EPA - but it might include the criteria used in determining the ratings.

Again - the site would not attempt to duplicate resources already available but provide links to the educational materials, games etc. - in short basically an educational portal.

Campaign

Because the labels would be applied to products that comply (albeit to different extents) the label becomes a visible, recognisable link between product, consumer and recycling.

Questions requiring further input:

What sort of criteria should be used for each rating?
See Andrew Allen's concept E-Rating for possibilities here

For example - does anyone have expertise on toxicity and harmful levels of various elements and compounds typically included in electronic products?

Can we establish some objective tests on reusability, repairability and recyclability?

Feel free to contribute answers - or pose some additional questions.

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

The underlying data requirements provided by the manufacturer and administered by the EPA will provide vital information regarding the materials contained in devices.
This will assist in the safe reprocessing of all devices according to appropriate practices.

It may be that an additional indicator (a red line perhaps) across a penguin body indicates items with ratings below the line are too hazardous for casual dis-assembly.

Consumers are informed (in the same way as energy rating) about which devices are safer, more repairable/(reusable) and recyclable.
The label, branding and imagery make links between purchase and disposal choices.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

As with energy labeling it will encourage consumers to make appropriate choices in purchasing equipment but now the whole family will be encouraged to make wise choices. This should also inspire manufacturers to achieve high ratings.
The label indicates how the product will be dealt with in the recycling process.
It will inform the younger generation with appropriate educational opportunities and the links from the website becoming a resource for individual and group project work, school resources. It does not replace existing resources but complements and enables them.

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

The domain (recycoolable.org) has already been created and can be developed/transferred as appropriate.
(Although I will endeavour to prototype something on free hosting again).
There are numerous design opportunities available to make the concept viable in the marketplace. Although penguins have been used for universal appeal it could be varied according to local, national or cultural needs.
I would welcome additional or alternative design material - my design skills are limited so input from anyone/everyone else is useful.

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

The EPA already has expertise in developing the energy star/rating system. The methods and controls utilised for that initiative are appropriate to this - it is not re-inventing the wheel but simply redesigning it for a different purpose.

 Similarly the EPA already has a database for energy efficiency so this would require some addition or modification to provide the underlying data (or links to it) in a central place for access by recycling organisations.
The raw data would be provided by the manufacturers from information they already have control over - even if devices are modularised and built from components supplied by a number of manufacturers the information is still available and an appropriate rating can be established.

My Virtual Team

The concept arises from a large range of inspirations that will gradually be acknowledged. My virtual team will be built accordingly .
The concept will no doubt need considerable modification to make it marketable so any contribution will be gratefully accepted - I shall find a place for collaborative development (assuming some people are happy to contribute).
