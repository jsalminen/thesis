Background

I recently read in a New York Times article: “More than 278 million mobile devices lie idle or deactivated in the United States, and nearly half are smartphones, consultants at Compass Intelligence say.” Mobile devices account for the majority of our ewaste, and most of them are smartphones. Something has to change, and it’s unlikely that the “two-years-maximum lifespan” notion will change as long as companies continue to make money. So let’s look at what we can do with these “obsolete” devices that still have plenty of life in them.

I was inspired by James Moyer’s idea for having companies make the programming on these devices open-source after 2 years. That way, others can come in and re-work the software so that the hardware can be utilized. Ultimately this idea to “open the hood” will reduce the number of devices recycled or thrown away; it will save consumers money when they buy “old” devices for new uses; and it will foster a much-needed innovative mentality with electronics.

Rewire

After I had thought of several ways people could reuse old smartphones, I started thinking more broadly. I thought about education. Google Scholar has a motto, “Stand on the shoulders of giants.” What if students were able to take a look under the hood at the code behind the previous generation of all smartphones? They would not only be able to learn in a very practical setting, but they would also be able to innovate.

Then I thought about an entrepreneurial group in my university, about OpenIDEO, and about other organizations that use collaboration to innovate. The result was rewire.com.

Rewire.com is my idea for a collaborative innovation site, dedicated to saving electronics from the dump. Rewire is a perpetually-funded program, operating on small returns from previous, successful projects. It’s not a business—although it does take care of its own. Instead, the rewire staff are individuals who are passionate about the cause.

The rewire community

The online community is made up of anyone with an open and innovative mind who wants to do something about our current e-waste crisis.

Partnering with a university (or universities)

The ideal would be for rewire to partner with one or more universities.  This would not only help with the funding and staffing, but it would also ensure the spirit of rewire is maintained because money is rarely the motivation for teachers.  In addition, a university would have the ability to integrate the rewire program into its course offerings, thus providing ample participation.  This would not only greatly help as the site gets its start, but it would provide the necessary participation going forward.

The process - See attached flowchart of process

The rewire process begins when someone has an idea. They present the idea to the online community (without revealing the “recipe”), and network with other users. They form a team of people who are all dedicated to the idea.

At this point, the team can go one of two ways: it can do its own thing or can team up with rewire. If it chooses to go its own way, they can raise funds through means such as kickstarter.com and don’t need to worry about any obligation to rewire. Rewire was simply happy to help connect the individuals.

If funding is of concern (which, for those of us without rich acquaintances to back us, it will be) then rewire is there to help. The team presents the idea to rewire and, if they like it, the staff will provide the necessary funding. In addition, the staff will assist with and facilitate all the planning that will need to take place—business details will need to be worked out, a prototype built, and markets researched. Once all that has been taken care of, the team can go find success. All that will be owed to rewire is a small percentage of the profits—a contract can work out all the intricate legal stuff—so that future innovators can receive that necessary monetary boost. 

A possible example of innovation
An old smartphone could be used as a home media device. It may be "outdated" according to a cell phone carrier's commercials, but it's got a good amount of memory in it and a good processor. So someone, with its code now being open-source, can create and distribute/sell a program for it that will do just that -- make it into a media device you can plug into your TV/stereo unit, or your car. The idea is it would be different than a cell phone -- you would leave it where it is most of the time, and disconnect it only when you need to change out the media on it (if it doesn't have something like a mini-usb connection this program could utilize).

In your home stereo, the once-smartphone can play media from its own memory, or it can connect via your wireless network to a service such as Youtube or Hulu or Netflix or Amazon Prime or whatever. These phones have the ability to connect to HD TVs anyway, and it's simple (and cheap) enough to convert output from the audio (headphone) jack to a stereo.

In your car, this could sit in the trunk (where many stereo systems have you put your 5-disc CD changers) the whole time and your car stereo's controls (or in some cases, the controls on the steering wheel) can sort through the music/media.  (This is much like how you can now with an iPhone connected, only you wouldn't have the hassle of having to connect and disconnect every time you get in/out of the car.) I've seen many touch-screen stereo units before.  So, if you didn't want it in the trunk, the device has a touch screen and could be used as the stereo control -- to switch songs or whatever -- right where your existing volume control and clock are.

This is just a very rough sketch of a possible idea that could be used by one of the rewire.com users.  He or she could propose it to rewire, gain the necessary help, and, for example, develop a kit to sell.  (It could include a tweaked version of the device's current OS as well as the necessary cables, etc, and simple instructions. Individuals can then buy the kit, and transform their smartphones (thus reviving an otherwise dead/obsolete device, saving it from the dump).  The rewire user(s) then has(have) a successful business. 

This hypothetical situation is far from completely developed, but hopefully you get an idea of this proposed rewire process.

Justification

It used to be that you had to know someone to actually get anywhere sometimes. Now that the world has become so interconnected through modern technologies, the game has changed (at least somewhat). Today, a youtube video can make a person famous (and rich) in a matter of days, or even hour—today, hopeful businesses raise $5 million in a few days for their new gaming system idea through kickstarter. Our notion of recycling and reusing things needs to upgrade as well. These new mediums—social networking, electronic (and wireless) communication, internet businesses, etc—all prove that an idea like rewire is not only possible but will work.

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

The goal of this concept is to keep electronics from landfills by creating new uses for them. Fewer electronics in landfills means fewer health concerns for those with the task of recycling the sometimes-dangerous components.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

This concept gives electronics a second chance to be useful. (See the example in the description above.)

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

Start thinking of new ways to use your old smartphone!

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

This would need a team of people excited about the idea. It would also require someone with web design skills as well as an initial investment (for the domain name as well as to fund the first projects). This could also benefit by partnering with a university or other such institution, as well as some advertising. :)
