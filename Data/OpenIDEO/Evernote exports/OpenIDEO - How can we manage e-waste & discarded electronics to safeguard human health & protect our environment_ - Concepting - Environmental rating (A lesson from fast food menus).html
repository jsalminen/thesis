Environmental rating (A lesson from fast food menus)

Modeled after restaurants with calories on the menus, electronics can be similarly assigned an "e-rating" for customers to see.

A trend which has been growing in popularity lately is the addition of calories to the menus at our favorite restaurants.  (As you can tell, I'm a Chipotle fan, so I've included a couple shots of their menus with calories.)  The idea for the "calorie menus" is to help make the consumer more aware.

Rating systems work
In a similar manner, electronics can be assigned a rating, based on a number of environmental criteria.  There are already rating systems out there, such as EPEAT.  In the news recently, Apple withdrew from the EPEAT rating system but changed its mind after backlash by loyal customers.  Customers are aware about the environmental impact of their purchases, and it needs to be considered heavily by electronics companies.

Proposed E-rating
Just like the calorie menus, the E-rating of a product would be displayed wherever consumers can see the product details for electronics when making purchases.  As an example, look at the screenshot of amazon.com that I've included. In the Product Details section of the page is where an e-rating would appear, for example.  

The e-rating would provide a score for the individual product as well as a score for the company.

The factors of the E-rating
The e-rating would be based on many factors.  Some might include (but there are certainly many more): the recyclability of the materials used, the amount of non-renewable resources used in production, the life expectancy of the electronic, and the amount of hazardous materials it contains.

Update: Daniel Shapiro pointed out the example of Timberland's green rating (see attached picture).  They use three rating criteria:
1. Climate Impact
2. Chemicals Used
3. Resource consumption
This model is certainly one to follow, as it is simple and to-the-point.  I also like how there is a composite score as well.  (Although I'm still in favor of a lowest-score-is-the-composite-score policy mentioned below...but this one may be more practical.)

Scoring procedures prevent companies from cheating
The e-rating would be based on several factors, each bringing a sub-score to the table.  The lowest sub-score would determine the overall e-rating (instead of the composite score).  For inspiration, I looked to another example of a rating system currently in place: the Windows Experience rating system. While this rates the "windows experience" of a PC based on factors such as the processor, graphics card, and RAM, we can apply some of those ideas here as well: an e-rating would take several factors into consideration and provide a score based on the lowest of those sub-scores.  This would ensure companies weren't able to score really high in all areas but one and still have a good score.

Implementation of e-rating
There are several ways this could be implemented.  The calorie menus are in place because of laws, so that's one option.  

Another option is to get the backing of a prestigious seller (such as amazon.com or Best Buy) to include the new rating system in the product details.  Such a partnership would eventually have a domino effect on other retailers, thanks to the power of the eco-conscious consumer's dollar.  I prefer this option.  The company will gain recognition as an environmentally-minded company while economic pressure will be placed upon makers of electronics.  The makers can shape up to stay in business, and  they now have a whole new field to compete on.

This will be new to consumers, of course.  Next to any e-rating online would be a "What's this?" button which would further inform the consumer of the e-rating program.  In stores, price tags would have e-ratings and descriptions of the program would be available.

Consumers apply the pressure
The market is in control, and the good news is the consumer cares.  As the news article I shared points out, consumers are more and more aware of the environmental impact of their decisions. A system like the e-rating system would flourish and the planet wouldn't be the only one benefiting: competition would drive down the price of more eco-friendly electronics so that consumers wouldn't need to pay more to appease their conscious; and companies would put more energy into "green innovation."

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

This makes consumers more aware of the environmental impact of their purchases while making our electronics more green.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

This encourages consumers to change their purchasing behavior, makes people more aware of e-waste, and inspires makers of electronics to make their products last longer and/or make them more e-friendly.

In conjunction with the recy-cool-ability concept proposed earlier, I think this is right on track. While we can't forcibly change people's minds, we can influence their decisions by providing them with relevant information.

This e-label is very similar to eco labels devised by some of the clothing manufacturers to highlight the carbon and chemical impacts generated during clothing production. Not only are significant amounts of water used, but harsh chemicals are often not completely filtered before disposal.

For communication, the simpler the better. When people scan packaging or lists, you only have a split second to catch their attention. A great example to use as a model is Timberland's "Green Index," which rates their products' production and waste footprint (no pun intended) only three things using a 10-point scale:
1) Climate Impact
2) Chemicals Used
3) Resource Consumption

A simple search for "Timberland" and "Green Index" yields plenty of great information about how they went through several iterations before they got the label right.
