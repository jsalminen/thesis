Mission #5 Surprise Us Find out more...

True impact is more than truthy impact.

Business certainly can - and must - be part of lasting social change. But let's not forget that the marketing of "social good" isn't always good for society. Is socially-active business possible? Sure. But we have to hold them to it.
Philosopher Slavoj Zizek talking about charity -- and how it can go wrong.
It used to be that socially-motivated corporations just gave to nonprofits -- whether through their corporate foundation, or through the founder/CEO's personal giving (e.g., Gates, Kellogg, & Ford Foundations). 

But today's businesses get involved in social good in all kinds of ways.

Some pursue better labor, environmental, ethical, or local-citizenship practices: See: Starbucks' Fair Trade coffee, Mead's post-consumer waste notebooks, restaurants' cage-free eggs, every car company's "Made in America" cars.

Some let consumers get involved, saying they'll donate money if consumers buy their products -- "Help us help the world", they say, and we do. See: TOMS shoes, a range of companies with Product RED, Kellogg's Box Tops for Education.

A lot of these campaigns are inspiring. A lot of them are profitable. So we can say they're successful examples of pairing profit with a social marketing. But there is still another step to move to social impact: accountability for results.

We know the old model of foundations can work: the Gates Foundation -- privately managed but the product of Microsoft wealth -- is single-handedly changing the world of public health. 

Can we say that the new models of social business do the same thing?

In designing this new solution to help reward businesses for social good, I challenge everyone here on OpenIDEO to determine the difference between business who are truly doing good and those who just talk like they do. 

Let's start with discussing what true impact by businesses looks like. Is it just a Fair Trade option among 15 others? Is it donating 1 cent per $2 item? Or is it more?


Two attachments:
1. In the video, philosopher Slavoj Zizek talks about how charity can hide corporate crime -- to a cartoon! It's a serious-and-seriously-entertaining reminder of how much fundamentals -- good corporate citizenship -- matter.
2. In the article in Stanford Social Innovation Review, professor Angela Eikenberry talks about what cause marketing means for nonprofits -- how it can sometimes be less than a good thing. http://www.ssireview.org/articles/entry/the_hidden_costs_of_cause_marketing

I don't think we need to be against consuming, necessarily, to worry that it's easy for companies to pretend to do good, even when they're not.

I think the 'alternative' is an independent credential -- a set of standards, or evaluation criterion. I wonder if we could regulate what counts as social impact business in the way we regulate 501c3s and 501c4s.
