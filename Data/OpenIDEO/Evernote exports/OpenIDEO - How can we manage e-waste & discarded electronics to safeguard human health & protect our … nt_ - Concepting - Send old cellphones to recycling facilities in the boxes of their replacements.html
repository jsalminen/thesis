Send old cellphones to recycling facilities in the boxes of their replacements

When individuals buy a new cellphone, they’re left with the phone, manuals, a box, and packing materials. Unfortunately, most consumers throw away these boxes and packing materials. Why not use them to return the cellphones being replaced?

Companies that engage in creative recycling programs for cellphones already exist. However, their challenge is to get the used products from consumers to their facilities. This model is intended to integrate with these existing companies, taking advantage of their past investment in recycling technologies. One of the main benefits of this model is that it incorporates existing materials and facilities, moving toward a closed-loop system of waste management.

This model is meant to involve both suppliers and consumers in a simple, low-cost method for recycling used cellphones and reducing packaging and shipping waste. It takes advantage of the existing materials made available upon purchase of a replacement product and uses them as the catalyst to spur action. Included in the package would be a pre-printed mailing sticker (much like electronic retailers already do for purchase returns) addressed to the nearest e-waste recycler. Among the information provided in the new package, a simple note would reach out to consumers explaining the basic premise of this concept:

"Now that you're happily enjoying your new '####,' why not dispose of your older phone instead of having it sit around, collecting dust? Simply place old devices with or without their chargers and accessories into the box, peel off the pre-printed mailing sticker found inside and drop it off next time you're near a post office."

There are three main obstacles facing this model: getting someone to pay for postage and physically getting the packages into a post office.

If consumers are forced to pay for postage, adoption rates of this program will likely be low. Two alternatives present themselves right away: the manufacturer could pay for postage (ultimately passing this cost along to consumers through higher sales prices) or a third-party (whether it be a government entity or a NFP) could adopt this initiative and pay for postage through grants or other fundraising means.

By providing pre-paid postage, eliminating the need to seek out a shipping box, and by including a letter explaining the benefits of recycling, this model tries to address the inertia present in most consumers.

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

Most importantly, it reduces the quantity of cell phones being thrown into the trash, instead contributing to the growing e-waste recycling industry.
Furthermore, since it uses mostly shipping and packaging materials that will already have been used, it does not create significant waste as a side effect.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

It fits both in the beginning and end of the lifecycle of electronic devices. At the beginning of a new cellphone's lifecycle, this solution offers a way to send used packaging to a recycling plant. At the same time, this solution provides a means for suitable disposal of used electronics at the end of their lives.

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

A list of existing electronics recycling companies could be gathered with the intent of reaching out to them to set up partnerships moving forward.
A list of relevant non-profit organizations and government agencies could be gathered with the intent of reaching out to them to seek out potential funds to provide postage.

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

The physical resources needed would be the mailing slips and the letters, both included in the boxes.
The monetary resources needed would be the cost of the two items mentioned above, and the cost of postage.
Partnerships with recycling companies, manufacturers, and potential sponsors would be key to ensuring the project's success.
Lastly, there may be alternate resource (physical, monetary, and man-power) needs in order to develop an associated awareness campaign.
The main obstacles to implementing and scaling this concept are negotiations with producers in order to determine willingness to pay for postage of these packages and negotiations with potential third-parties to determine their willingness to adopt this concept.
