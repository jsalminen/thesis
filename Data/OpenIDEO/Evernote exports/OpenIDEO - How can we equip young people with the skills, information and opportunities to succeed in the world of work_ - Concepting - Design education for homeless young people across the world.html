Design education for homeless young people across the world

Through a design curriculum that helps homeless youth. We can provided them with a basic understanding of industrial, graphic and architectural design skills that could be applied in their everyday lives and can serve of inspiration for a job.
Two Week Curriculum for a youth homeless shelter in Colombia
iDo Colombia is a design curriculum that I developed to help homeless teenagers in Bogotá, Colombia. My aim was to provide the teenagers with a basic understanding of industrial, graphic and architectural design skills that could be applied in their everyday lives. By creating an understanding of the design process, I aimed to help them identify, analyze and solve design problems, while also helping them address personal challenges. To achieve this, the curriculum focused on the expression of emotions with the ultimate goal of helping the teenagers to learn potential new and enjoyable ways to earn a living. In addition, the curriculum was designed to foster environmental awareness and teamwork.

The curriculum was developed between Fall 2010 and Spring 2011 and was inspired by iDo (Industrial Design Outreach). It became a branch of iDo and with the guidance of iDo’s mentors it was finalized and given its ultimate name. During June and July 2011, I delivered iDo Colombia at two emergency centers of the Children of the Andes Foundation in Bogotá, Colombia. Leveraging the insights from the two-month program, I have re-structured iDo Colombia to help educators and designers continue to use design as a tool for social change.

How will your concept support young people as they transition into the world of work?

First of all, I am focusing on homeless young people which certainly have different challenges than young people with families.
Through design projects young homeless people could re-gain their self-worth and believe in themselves. They would develop skills that could apply at places that focus of manufacturing. Once they know of different forms of making a living, they would seek diverse opportunities and try to get educated.
I worked with 48 young men and after the workshops, many of them manifested their interest in further developing their education and seeing internships related to design.

What online or in-person components of your concept will best support this transition?

Mentoring
Networking with design agencies willing to provide temporary internships once the students gain skills
Community support
Educational projects
Follow up support from mentors and peers

Tamar can you elaborate a little more in the question regarding the items? what exactly do you mean by items?

I followed a process starting by introducing the teens to the world of design. You see to most of them, the term design is completely alien. Once they knew what design was, they explored how their lives where affected by design. While in that process most of them started finding what part of design they identified with and what part of their lives could inspire such identification. After that I introduced them to the challenge and they started the ideation process followed by prototyping.

Because of the precarious conditions of the emergency centers where these teens lived, the curriculum was delivered only for two weeks, and repeated twice with different groups. It may seem short, but I believe it made an impact. I spent approximately $1,500 in materials. Also, we used reclaimed materials and hundreds of plastic lids donated by companies. Before heading to Colombia, I hosted a fundraising event that gave me $600. iDo at SF State University donated $400 and I put $500 of my own money plus my time and the coverage of all my travel and living expenses.

Ideally, it would require affiliation with a design school as under perfect conditions, this curriculum could be constantly delivered. You see the teenagers live in this place for a period of 3 to 4 weeks. That means that if there are two pilot 2 week curriculums, they could be re-delivered once a month by a different group of designers on a different group of students.

I think for this challenge, it would be wonderful if the curriculum was delivered by people already in the industry. That is designers or firms willing to give a percentage of their time to teach design. That way the teens could get a glimpse of the real design world and perhaps be given an opportunity as interns.
