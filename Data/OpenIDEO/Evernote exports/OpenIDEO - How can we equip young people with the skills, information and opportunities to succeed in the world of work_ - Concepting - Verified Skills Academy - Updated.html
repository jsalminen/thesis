Verified Skills Academy - Updated

A multi-tiered on-line service focused on allowing employers to identify the skills that they most want and then for students to acquire and showcase those skills to employers.
 
 
 
Example Use case:
Annie is worried about her preparedness for the world post-school and in her search for options, she stumbles upon "Verified Skills Academy". She sees that a lot of employers have expressed an interest in hiring those with graphic design/drawing skills. Not only that, she discovers that a low number of people already have those necessary skills! This represents a great opportunity to develop a marketable skill in an area where demand is exceeding supply.


Annie then sets off going through training modules and takes basic diagnostic tests to "verify" the skills and knowledge she's acquired. As she learns, she begins to post things she has made to her portfolio. Others have rated her work highly, one piece she uploaded even has an average rating of 8/10. Big Corp is looking for a new designer and searches for those with demonstrated graphic design capabilities. They are presented with Annie as an option and so they look through her work and at her "knowledge map" that shows all of the training she has had and tests she has passed. They love her work and she has verified Photoshop and InDesign skills. Big Corp wastes no time and makes contact. After interviewing her and a few other top candidates, they select Annie as a new graphic designer!


Components of website/service:

A skills and knowledge registry: Employers can "like" certain skills and areas of knowledge denoting their desire for employees with those skills and knowledge. This would feed into a "leader-board" of marketable skills and knowledge that students would be able to see. Thus they could focus their learning on the most marketable skills. Everyone would also be able to see how many people already have that skill and knowledge. This could encourage people to go towards the areas where there is the biggest disconnect between supply and demand.
Instructional Materials: This would include videos, games, simulations, etc. focused on teaching the skills and knowledge from the registry. The leader-board would allow the developers to focus on making learning modules for the the most marketable skills and knowledge.
Validation and Testing: Students would be able to take tests intended to demonstrate whether or not they have certain skills and knowledge. This would "verify" capabilities. This improves upon the typical resume as employers can't tell unless they give a prospective employee a test whether they can actually perform certain skills (e.g. program in Java). To differentiate between levels of skill, a % will be displayed based on how many sub-modules and sub-tests have been completed. Test scores will also be displayed to convey the degree of understanding.
Web of Knowledge: Based off of what Khan Academy does, each student could build up their web of knowledge by going through the modules and by taking associated tests. This web of knowledge would be open so that employers could reach out to those with the capabilities they are looking for. Each knowledge or skill would have four levels. 
  1. Blank - no test taken or learning module experienced.

  2. Yellow - Claims to have the associated skill/knowledge but unverified.
  3. Blue - Gone through the associated learning modules
  4. Green - Student has taken the associated test and so the knowledge or skill is "verified"
The web of knowledge could be formatted into a CV for the students.

Matching: Students would be able to search for employers that are looking for those with their skill set and employers would be able to search for those that have the skills they are looking for.
Mentorship and Tutoring: Students could ask for help and be matched with volunteer tutors and mentors who can help them through the modules and provide advice and help on skills that cannot be put into on-line learning modules easily (e.g. dealing with interviews).
 
 

How will your concept support young people as they transition into the world of work?

Students will know that they are focusing on marketable skills and the website will facilitate them acquiring and showcasing those skills. The transition to the world of work will also be facilitated as they can search for employers based on their demonstrated ("verified") knowledge and skills.
 

What online or in-person components of your concept will best support this transition?

The combination of the leader-board of desired skills and instructional modules to learn those skills will allow students to be prepared for the transition. The web of knowledge and generated CV will allow them to showcase their skills and knowledge.

I like the idea of employers communicating the skills they seek. A design issue to pursue further is how to address the fact that many skills employers may seek are not like yes-no questions but rather ones where how good you are relative to other applicants is a legitimate criterion for the employer to consider. An employer may want the best writer from among the applicants (rather than an adequate writer) or the most proficient economist, or the one most likely to make good judgments under pressure, or the fastest learner.

Thank you for the feedback! Very true. The binary system described has very large limitations. I'm still wrestling with how to develop an effective ranking system. For some skills, it is possible to break it into small chunks that can be made yes/no (programming is an example) for at least the beginner to intermediate stages of skill acquisition. This could result in a % being displayed for each user that could provide an idea of rank. For more subjective skills like writing, a rating system (by users, employers, experts, someone) would have to be overlaid or some algorithm would have to run each student's portfolio to determine the "quality".
