Mission #5 Surprise Us Find out more...

All work is honorable (and we shouldn't be afraid of that)

Education and society must abandon the image of "success equals a white collared shirt." How are we supposed to grow our specialized labor industries if these people might feel looked down on because they didn't force themselves into an office job?
 
Mike Rowe, star of
One thing that stands out to me as a young person beginning his career, is that society seems to have grown a disturbing level of disdain for skilled labor jobs. I'd say I've experienced this firsthand; I completed college because personally I would feel like I missed out on something in life if "all" I did was become a plumber or a construction worker or the like. But why do I feel this way?



A couple of years ago, I worked on a project designing a new product for construction sites. I interviewed many workers and contractors, and honestly expected them to be largely uneducated, unintelligent people who did their job because they couldn't do anything else. I was completely in the wrong. These guys are sharp, and some are downright brilliant; contractors especially showed great ability to think through problems quickly.



But there is a certain image assigned to certain professions in our society, isn't there? I'm worried, especially in our transitioning global economy, that we're unknowingly eroding the skill sets of certain professions by making people who would otherwise excel at the job feel somewhat ashamed to be doing it, as if "society" expects them to do better.



I hope this is making sense. Mike Rowe, in the attached video, speaks before Congress and addresses some of this in his remarks. When I discovered this video a few months ago, it really opened my eyes and changed a lot of my thinking about certain jobs.


If education is going to evolve to suit the needs of tomorrow, it has to do a better job of identifying people suited to these specialized skill sets early, cater to their intellectual needs, and not treat them second-rate for not eventually working in an office or wearing a white collared shirt. We've all heard the joke of the teacher who staples the McDonald's application to a student's failed test. We can't afford to find that sort of thing funny anymore.
