Reflecting on E-Waste

Yesterday I spent a lot of time learning and thinking about e-waste.
Television left next to a trash can at the train station.

Yesterday I had the chance to tour Green Citizen, a local e-waste recycling center near my house. I was inspired to meet with them after reading through the Inspiration Mission related to hosting an interview. You can read what I learned â€“ and you can find out how to host your own interview!

In any event, I spent the morning learning about e-waste and all of the great things Green Citizen is doing to help recycle, reuse and refurbish old electronics. As I left their office, I felt inspired, excited and hopeful for the future. I walked to the local public transit depot to take a train to the office, and as I approached the station, I saw this television next to the trash can. Clearly someone no longer needed this TV and so they just left it by the trash.

I was so struck by the contradiction I was seeing and feeling. Here you have a company that's striving to provide consumer education and recycling services to keep electronics out of landfills. And less than a half mile away, here was this TV just waiting to be taken to the dump!

As we move into Concepting, I'm inspired to think about ideas that educate consumers to help them do the right thing. Whoever dropped the TV at the garbage could have just as easily driven a few minutes further and done an incredible and responsible thing for our environment and for our community. How could we have helped steer this person to a place like Green Citizen instead?

Here our municipal councils have an annual collection of household items too large to put into ordinary trash - some get refurbished and resold from the municipal depot and I think others go to recyclers (but we aren't told if this is the case). Of course this still means all the small items still get trashed.
Sims is a worldwide recycling firm (originally just metal but now including e-waste) and I suspect they are the final repository for our material, they have a large but I guess unheralded presence in USA.
As Scott points out the issue is demand - as Mike Biddle points out in his inspiration ( http://www.openideo.com/open/e-waste/inspiration/responsible-recycling-of-the-difficult-material-to-recycle-from-e-waste-plastics/ ) there is strong resistance to recycled material for input to manufacturing (despite improving cost advantages). In the case of plastics perhaps the demand can be improved through growth in the micro-manufacturing industry exemplified in 3D printing and modeling.
Terracycle emphasizes upcycling (although the environmental movement would call it down-cycling) as a means of reusing some materials whereas companies like Mike Biddle's MBA Polymers look to true recycling.

I absolutely agree it is a case of education being required but I think more than that we need a way to make it 'cool' to buy items that have a high percentage of recycled product. Perhaps some of the consumer electronics firms could offer locally customised casings for their products or better still personally designed 'skins'. In one of the other inspirations there was a suggestion for releasing parts designs for repair - maybe the purchase price of a new smart phone could include a choice of locally manufactured casings or the design to make your own.
