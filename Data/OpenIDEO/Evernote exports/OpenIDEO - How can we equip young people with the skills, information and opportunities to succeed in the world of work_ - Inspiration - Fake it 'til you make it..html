Fake it 'til you make it.

Underrated soft skill: being able to pick what you want to do and tell a convincing story about how you chose. Paralysis of indecision can be a much greater risk than making the wrong decision.
 
123rf stock photo -
 
Two things I should have believed when I graduated two years ago:
1. Very few people end up staying on a single "path" their whole lives.
2. The most important part of making your first career move, is just to make one. You'll get more hard skills and perspective in your first year of working than any class will teach you. 


The only real difference between my friends who had jobs after graduation and those that didn't was their ability to decide in the first place. The people who seemed like they had it all figured out, really just decided to figure it out. 2 things that make this difficult: knowing what the options are in the first place, and then articulating convincingly how you picked one.


Most self-aware 22 year olds know that they have very few distinguishable skills or traits to bring to the workforce. They end up aimlessly shooting off their resumes and generic cover letters to the few places everyone else is, in the hopes that the perfect fit will reveal itself. The result? Recent grads all apply to the same finance/consulting jobs and have unconvincing stories about why they want to do anything over anything else. In an article he wrote last week, David Brooks talks about why finance and consulting are such popular paths. The prestige is obviously part of it, but another factor is the clarity and structure of the path - a safe and comfortable bet. (These are also the primary sectors recruiting at "blue state" universities, as Brooks refers to them.) 


Successful candidates from my college friend pool are successful navigators of the options, but they are also good storytellers. No one ever told a great story about a comfortable bet. Successful candidates choose a starting point, point to something in their past experience that validates their choice, and can see both short term and long term steps.
