Golden E-Waste: a commodity more valuable than gold (ore)

We propose a solution: to create a model in which financial and moral incentives intersect: precious metals can be extracted from E-Waste. Pascal Krummenacher & Franklyn Zhu
 
 
 

The Situation

We believe that when bringing about change, leaders, companies, governments, and idealists alike need to understand the market they are trying to influence. Make no mistake, we live in a world of economics, every action on every stage of the economic chain can be explained through incentive analysis. When it comes to green e-waste management, we do not believe that we can rely on the inherent “goodness” of people to bring them to reduce, reuse, or recycle.

Perhaps the solution is simply to create a model in which financial and moral incentives happen to intersect. Perhaps the solution lies within the problem. Consider:



Background to our Solution

E-Waste is a valuable commodity.

People often believe that recycling is expensive and unrealistic. But the proliferation of recycling companies in the last few decades seems to show the contrary. Look at a company like the China Metal Recycling company (profit of 10 billion HKD in 2011); E-Waste, rather than being a huge environmental inconvenience, can actually be a valuable commodity.

Consider an old mobile phone. According to a 2009 UNEP Report on E-Waste, “Metals represent on average 23% of the weight of a phone”, including elements such as copper, tin, and special metals such as cobalt, indium, antimony, and precious metals such as gold, silver, and palladium. Of course, these metals are present in individual phones only in trace amounts, but in one ton of mobile phones (without batteries), this averages out to be 3.5 kg of silver, 340 g of gold, 140 g of Palladium, and 130 kg Copper.

In fact, “E-waste is often richer in rare metals than their ores, containing 10 to 50 times higher copper content than copper ore. A cell phone contains five to 10 times higher gold content than gold ore.” (Source: http://articles.timesofindia.indiatimes.com/2007-06-08/india-business/27973905_1_e-waste-generation-satish-sinha-ores)

E-‘Waste’? We think not. This is clearly a commodity, that if properly processed, can be extremely profitable.



There is no shortage of E-Waste

Technology is evolving at an exponential pace, often outdated within months. Consumers are purchasing electronic products at increasing rates, crying out for newer, faster, and better. “According to the UN Environmental Program, some 20 to 50 million metric tons of e-waste are generated worldwide every year” (Source: Project brief)

In developing countries like China, India, Brazil, there is such an overabundance of E-Waste (imported from countries like the US, Great Britain) and such an dearth of treating facilities. India experienced 450% growth of E-Waste volume in a mere 7 years (Source: http://www.pluggd.in/electronic-waste-in-india-297/) There simply is no lack of this commodity, and given the way technology is evolving, given our increasing purchasing power, there doesn’t seem to be any shortage of E-Waste in the foreseeable future.



Obtaining metals from recycling is more environmentally friendly than primary production (mining)

According to the UNEP report, for every 1 ton of gold, palladium, or platinum produced, over 10,000 tons of CO2 emissions are generated. Conversely, recycling these materials from E-Waste produces only a fraction of the greenhouse emissions of primary production. 



We believe that this is a window for profit in recycling E-Waste. This is what the recycling process would look like:



Collection

Simply put, we will purchase e-waste in bulk. There is currently very little market for old outdated machinery, meaning there is a great window of opportunity to be pioneers in the e-waste market.

E-Waste can be collected in many ways.

a) By e-waste collectors, from whom we will purchase E-waste. This creates jobs and revenue for the unemployed and homeless. We need to set up points of collection in big cities, and from there transfer e-waste to our central recycling facilities. We believe this is viable; for instance, in Beijing, there is a very active plastic bottle collecting community. Poor, old, unemployed people who have found no other means of breadwinning have chosen this job. In Beijing, recycling facilities will purchase plastic bottles at 0.1 RMB per bottle (according research that I have personally done). E-Waste, although is much more scarce than plastic material, can be potentially much more lucrative than bottle collecting, and may galvanize collectors to be more proactive (perhaps even go up to people’s houses to collect).

b) By electronics stores. We could make deals with big electronic companies (such as Radio Shack in the US, Da Zhong Dian Qi in China, Fortress in Hong Kong etc). They buy old electronics, and we in turn buy it from them with a slight difference. This makes it easier for owners of old electronics to get rid of their e-waste (and get it to us)



Dismantling

This step involves a bit of manpower. Batteries need to be taken from electronics. Processors, microchips need to be dismantled from the frames of the waste electronics. CRT screens, PVC material needs to be isolated to prevent pollution in later stages. But in a developing country where manpower is cheap, this can be (and is being) done.



Processing

This involves the shredding of e-waste into smaller pieces, and then the extraction of precious metals from the e-waste. This requires advanced, but absolutely viable technology (we will see it’s viable in a later case study about ERI). The process must be precise, as unwanted mixture of metals will lead to impure metal products, decreasing the value of our output. The extracted metals need to be smelted as well, meaning we would need to build our own smelting plants.



Back to consumers!

There is huge demand for metals, and the supply from primary production is getting scarcer by the day. The only possible alternative, if we are to keep using metals, is to recycle (unless we can mine from a conveniently placed asteroid right by our planet—but that’d be quite expensive wouldn’t it?).

We realize that this involves the set up of massive, complicated logistics. However, we believe that there is a huge market for recycling e-waste and extracting raw materials. Apart from e-waste being a commodity, there is also substantial supporting evidence to our belief.



Case Study: ERI

The American company ERI (Electronic Recyclers International) was founded in 2002. It does exactly what we have been talking about, and as of 2011 turns over some $45 million profit per year (http://news.medill.northwestern.edu/chicago/news.aspx?id=183350). They expect to make $100 million in profit in 2012. Of course, there’s a hitch: some US states provide subsidies for every ton of e-waste recycled. These subsidies made up $33.6 million of ERI’s total $45 million profits in 2010.

But that means they still made some $10 million US dollars—despite the fact they are based in the US, where manpower is so expensive. In Asia, in Brazil, in Africa, our operations costs would be very low.



Recycling will eventually be absolutely necessary

The world is mining metals at a rapid rate. Demand is increasing, but supplies are decreasing. The only way to keep using metals on earth is to start to recycle. Governments are realizing this; policy will tend towards sustainable development when resources inevitably get scarce. There is no doubt about this. 

Just look at recent government policies. China is frantically trying to balance economic development with sustainability; it is a HUGE struggle within China. There might not be enough impetus for sustainable development now, but when our metal supplies run dangerously low, the sustainable path will be the only path left to mankind. By jumping into the recycling business, we are taking a huge step ahead of everyone else, and eventually, government policy will be fully behind us.

It is then that the subsidies (and millions) will really start pouring in. It is then that we will be able to expand our already existing infrastructure for recycling. It is then that the world will rely more on recycling our old metals, rather than mining an already depleted earth.



 
 

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

Recycling is more environmentally friendly than Primary Production (mining)

For instance, (according to the UNEP report of 2009) "production of 1 kg aluminium by recycling uses only 1/10 or less of the energy required for primary production", and produces less harmful byproducts.



Moreover, this project is a massive leap towards sustainability. As previously mentioned, world supplies of metals are running out. The only way to keep using metals is to recycle them.



It is acknowledged, however, that the IMPROPER treatment of e-waste can have very harmful results. The workers who dismantle the e-waste can easily be poisoned. CRT screens contain lead, and if are not treated properly can be detrimental to both human and environmental health. Coolants, gases used in refrigerators, can also be very harmful if not treated properly. Therefore, it is crucial that our working conditions in the recycling plants are up to standard, and that we plan every step of the actual recycling process.
 

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

We believe that it is absolutely important to change our consumeristic mindset and think more sustainably, however, we also think it is a little impractical at this stage to curb consumerism. We live in a capitalistic world of human beings: we like new things, we like buying things, we are consumers by nature. Our proposal simply finds a point where human financial incentive intersects with that which is also sustainable!



Furthermore, the idea itself fits sustainably in the overall economic chain. Why? Because we make money. A purely moral and educational campaign, although may eventually show results, is also black hole for money. Consider China's 'switch-old-for new' campaign: it was terminated at the end of 2011, after only a year of operation. Those buyback programs in Chinese electronics stores also stopped, because the companies relied on government subsidies and did not build a sustainable recycling infrastructure. Our project aims to BUILD this infrastructure.
 

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

This is a huge idea; as individuals who do not specialize in this area, we have not thought out all the ramifications of this idea, and so we ask all OpenIdeo members to contribute!



Some things we think that we should tackle are:



1. The actual process of recycling

There is existing technology that can do this. We need to figure out how to actually do this, the technical details. We need experts in recycling, in dismantling, in smelting.



2. IMPORTANT: how to sell this to investors.

We believe that this idea is 100% viable. Why? ERI is already doing it. And they're making millions. But the market for e-waste is young! We need to come up with concrete, 200% well supported reasons and evidence to convince investors that we can make the most out of this lucrative market. Without investors, none of this will be possible. Why? Because we need startup capital to build recycling plants. We need money to hire thousands of workers to sort this material. We need millions to buy the shredding technology, to build the smelting plants. We need money to set up the infrastructure of collecting e-waste.



At the end of the day, we believe that with enough dedicated people and enough investment capital, this is a sure chance for success. We believe there is a CLEAR opening in this young market. ERI is in the US; it's already hard enough for them to expand there given their operations costs. In China, in Brazil, in India, in developing countries, we could build up and catch up to ERI in only a few years! Plus, developing countries is where all the e-waste goes anyways, what better way to solve the problem than to make the problem our solution?!



3. We realize that some of the arguments made in this idea are not fully developed and supported. If any arguments seem specious or flawed, please point them out! And help do some research as well! (cite sources!)
 

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

1. Investment Capital

I'm really not sure how much this would cost, but it certainly would be a lot. I'm from China, and in China it takes about 50 million RMB ($7.8M) to build a generic, large animal feed factory. Given that we're going to need SMELTING equipment in addition to shredding technology, it will probably cost even more: around 80 million RMB ($12.6M) for a single plant in China? But of course, we would also need operating costs, build a technical team from scratch, and build an entire recycling infrastructure. I have never started my own company, but I'm anticipating at least some $20 million USD to get everything going.



2. Technology

Shredding technology is out there. Check out this news article: “Cerebra unveils e-waste mobile shredder”. (Source: http://articles.timesofindia.indiatimes.com/2011-03-14/infrastructure/28687573_1_e-waste-cerebra-integrated-technologies-mobile-phones-and-computers). This is a burgeoning field, and technology is certainly established (but not widespread). Check this out: ( http://www.alibaba.com/product-gs/489473356/MX_800_1250_large_capacity_professional.html). An industrial grade E-shredder online for order, about $100,000 per shredder. Capacity? 1-10 tons/hour. I believe with enough of these we could be getting quite some shredded e-waste per day.



3. Partnerships

It is not very realistic to build our own smelting plants, so perhaps it would be viable to strike deals with existing companies with smelting plants. Or perhaps, waste metal recycling companies? Vertical integration may maximize our control and profit, but given the scale of what we are proposing, perhaps it might not be that feasible. What do you all think?



4. Time

This is a window of opportunity, especially in developing countries like China. Here, we are still balancing between rapid development and sustainability, with most corporations still unsure how to maneuver. Here, we have companies sprouting like weeds, people wanting get-rich-quick schemes, who don't even consider sustainability. They make their money, pollute, and move to the US where they send their rich kids to school without any concern for the future of developing countries. What they fail to realize is that in the long run, quality counts. Consider the stock market in China: in the early 2000s, all the stocks were great. Any stock you bought made money. But now? It's not that easy. Now, quality actually counts; not any company can go public.



We have in front of us a window of opportunity. The quicker we can start, the better!

Welcome to OpenIDEO guys!



Agree that this is thoroughly researched and well presented argument.

As you say the costs of setting up a single recycling process is huge and of course there are some already in existence that, as you say, are turning a profit.

I'm interested in the economics involved (although I'm not an economist) - and I do agree there is a window of opportunity to enter the recycling market profitably.

At present the profitability in recycling arises from the negligibly small cost of raw material input (i.e. the e-waste): being largely transportation cost (you can either pay employees to collect 'free' raw materials, pay casual collectors in lieu of collecting it yourself or pay consumers to deliver product to you).

Where is the tipping point - the price at which buying back say a typical mobile phone gives the smallest acceptable return on investment?



At the outset of this challenge I asked some questions of an inspiration contributor who is the CEO of an ABS plastic recycling firm (as he suggested the last, bulkiest and one of the more difficult components to recycle). From his replies it became apparent that everything has to be done on a very large scale to make it profitable - their 'product' is high grade recycled ABS suitable for feeding back into the manufacturing stream. Clearly they are profitable and partner with metal recovery firms in Britain, Europe and China. I broached the question of downscaling and was informed that they were looking at it from the perspective of pre-processing as it was presently not economic to consider small scale full-function recycling.



At the moment recycling profitability relies mainly on consumer 'goodwill' - that is goods returned for no cash benefit to the consumer.

Taking the ERI example a quick check of 2010 shows profit of $10m on turnover of 120m pounds of e-waste (figures from the article you quoted) - converting this to profit per 100g yields a figure of approximately 2 cents. My Samsung mobile phone weighs 108g (possibly including battery which of course is an input cost already factored into the profit).



At 2c per phone the industry will continue to require 'free' materials for input and there is no real commercial incentive for the consumer in returning goods as recycling raw materials.

This is not exactly the outcome I was expecting (or hoping for) when I began the exercise but it is useful to know.



Given that profitability presently relies on economies of scale the real opportunity lies in developing smaller, more efficient facilities that can compete (closer to their input markets).



Looking at the 'subsidies' raises a whole new range of figures that I will leave for another day.



Thanks again for your input and hope to see you in future challenges too.

Corporations, the major purchasers of electronic devices must be induced to actively manage their waste streams.



Based upon voluntary activity, manufacturer takeback programs are inconsistent with one another. Responsibility rests upon the organization to ensure that EOL electronics are managed using safe and environmentally sound processes. The R2 (Responsible Recycling) Standard addresses specific considerations such as: health and human safety, handling of components containing hazardous materials, and the legal transfer and trade of products. E-Stewards certification follows stricter requirements. In addition to the R2 Standard, recyclers must not only have ISO 14001 certification but also exclude toxic waste from landfills and incinerators as well as export to developing countries. To maintain certification, recyclers must additionally submit to at least one on-site audit per year.
