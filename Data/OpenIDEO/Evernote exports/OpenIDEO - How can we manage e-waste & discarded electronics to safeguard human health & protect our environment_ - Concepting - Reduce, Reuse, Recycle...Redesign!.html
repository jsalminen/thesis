Reduce, Reuse, Recycle...Redesign!

I view the problem of e-waste as having two basic components: (1) how to handle the large quantities of e-waste already in existence, and (2) how to move forward to eliminate the problem in the future. Each one requires its own solution.
 
 
Since the solutions needed to deal with currently existing e-waste and to design a better future moving forward are distinct, but interrelated, I'm going to structure my ideas in two parts: how to implement the reduce, reuse, recycle framework to deal with e-waste now, and how to think about re-designing electronics to eliminate the problem of e-waste in the future.


I. What to do about e-waste now
A. Reduce and Reuse
We can reduce the amount of e-waste by creating markets for the sale of used electronics that may still be in working condition. Got an upgrade on that two-year old cell phone? Why not re-sell it to someone who might want it (perhaps people on a student budget like myself!). Old laptop with a slower processor than your pretty new computer? Maybe a school could make use of it. Old chargers that don't work for any of your new devices? A company might be able to make use of the metal in the wires. 


I think online fora and marketplaces for the exchange of working electronics or components is a really important step in diminishing the rate at which e-waste makes its way to the landfill. Various concepts submitted in this challenge already get at how to get this underway: Ashwin Gopi's Hacker Cookbook concept is a great way of thinking about alternative uses for our electronics. David Payne's E-trash into real cash concept would make it easier for sellers of used electronics to find buyers -- creating value for everyone involved while Robert Suarez's Hack Nation would allow people to trade up their electronics. Paul Reader's RecyCOOLability concept and Ashley Jablow's E-waste bins idea would both make it easier for consumers to know where to take their electronics that don't have residual market value for processing. 


B. Recycle
We need to do everything possible to ensure that e-waste collected now is recycled in a responsible manner. Unfortunately, it is often hard to recover the individual raw materials from electronics, making it an unprofitable activity where labor costs are high and there are no mandates requiring recycling to be done domestically. As a result, there has been growing concern over the recycling of e-waste in countries where environmental and occupational safety regulations are not well-developed or well-enforced. This video on the now-infamous town of Guiyu in China shows exactly what can happen when e-waste recycling is done wrong: http://www.cbsnews.com/video/watch/?id=4586903n


Unsafe e-waste recycling practices can cause pollution and health problems for workers and residents. However, recycling is often a major source of income for people with few alternative economic options. Therefore, where e-waste recycling is being done unsafely, I think we should seek to help workers upgrade their practices by providing them with safety training and the equipment needed to manage e-waste properly. This approach has been used by the Blacksmith Institute to address mercury pollution caused by artisanal gold mining and processing (http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FIbhwaw5PQg&feature=relmfu). By installing simple but important technological improvements in workshops, the initiative coordinated by Blacksmith has helped to reduce pollution while maintaining jobs in local communities -- with local and global implications from the reduction of mercury pollution. 


What if we could do the same at sites where e-waste is recycled? Guiyu already took a step to improving its environment by requiring that plastic casing of wires be stripped from metal rather than burnt. This reduced air pollution and even though the process is more time and labor intensive than burning, it also meant that recyclers could sell the plastic to a factory for processing into other products. 


I think we could promote improvements in recycling practices by creating an open-source database of best practices for managing different types of e-waste: computer monitors, cathode-ray tubes, plastic casings etc. We could also seek to create easy 'kits' that could be distributed to people working that provides them with the necessary personal protective equipment etc. needed to do recycling appropriately. This database could also be linked with an online marketplace for materials from e-waste recycling such as plastics and metals to allow people to find the best buyers for their products. 


II. How to make the electronic future better
As several people have touched on already, I think the fundamental solution to the e-waste conundrum is rethinking how we design electronics now. As Valentina suggested, design with the idea of recycling and disassembly in mind can turn our 'extract --> build --> trash' model into a 'design --> use --> disassemble --> re-use' model, basically creating a circular ecosystem of electronic materials. 


I think some businesses and designers are already making moves in this direction, and I'm excited to see what other ways electronics can be made 'greener'. I also think this is an area where companies who want to do the right thing can be helped by good government policy. For example, the EU's waste electronics legislation sets mandatory targets for the collection of e-waste. If all companies have to collect and handle a certain amount of e-waste, those companies who have designed their products to be easier/cheaper to recycle will gain a competitive advantage. Also the incorporation of tracking information in the production of devices can help consumers and recyclers easily figure out how best to deal with a product at the end of its life. 


On a broader scale, governments could shift to some degree from taxing labor and sales (both activities that we think of as generally good things for the economy) to taxing/fining resource extraction and waste production. This would align government incentives with sustainable business goals and ease the transition from our current model to a cleaner, more efficient future. 
 
 

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

By addressing e-waste in all stages of it's lifecycle, my concept seeks to minimize future harms while also addressing the immediate dangers posed by unsafe e-waste disposal or recycling now.
 

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

My concept seeks to look at the coordinated actions needed to address the current volume of e-waste as well as how to transition to a better electronics design and use model in the future.
 

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

The following could be done by local, state or national governments, private companies or NGOs and foundations:

(i)Creating online marketplaces for sale of used electronics or components

(ii) setting up convenient collection sites for electronics

(iii) creating a database of e-waste recycling 'hotspots' -- i.e. places where e-waste is being recycled in an unsafe manner.

(iv) creating a database of the best practices for recycling e-waste so information could be distributed worldwide

(v) thinking about what essential materials people working in resource-limited settings would need to be able to carry out recycling safely



The following would require some sort of government action, or coordinated industry-wide agreements:

(i) requiring targets for the collection and processing of e-waste based on the volume of electronics produced by a company

(ii) incentivizing work and economic transactions rather than resource extraction and waste disposal through a re-alignment of government policy
 

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

This initiative could be implemented starting immediately, but will need to be phased-in over time to achieve the multiple objectives. The creation of online marketplaces and databases could be crowdsourced and continuously updated/revised based on changing knowledge. Partnerships between universities, design group and companies would be beneficial in re-thinking how we design electronics (especially as some of these efforts are likely to require specialized technical knowledge). In order to reach current sites that are being polluted by e-waste, a network of people on the ground who can reach out to workers would be necessary.
