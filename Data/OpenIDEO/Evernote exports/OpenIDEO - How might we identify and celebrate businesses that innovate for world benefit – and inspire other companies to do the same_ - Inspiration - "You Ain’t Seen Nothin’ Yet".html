"You Ain’t Seen Nothin’ Yet"

He's won the Turing Award, the National Medal of Technology, and the Presidential Medal of Freedom. He works at Google. He is what some call "a genius with repeat success." June 6th, 2012 he and Google and partners paved the way for everything.
Reverberations of Strengths. Cooperrider file.

IPv6 is Exponential Not Incremental<o:p></o:p>

 When the Internet launched operationally in 1983, its creators never dreamed that there might be billions of devices and users trying to get online. Yet now, almost three decades later, that same Internet serves nearly 2.5 billion people and 11 billion devices across the globe. And we've been running out of space. In order to connect to the Internet, each device has to have an IP address—a numerical label that identifies every computer, phone, tablet, eBook reader, etc. But like a telephone network that is running out of phone numbers, the current Internet was close to running out of IP addresses.

 On June 6th, 2012 Vince Cerf of Google, and Google as a whole, played a major role in founding the next major step for the internet which lays the groundwork for a smart planet with sensors that sense temperatures, vibrations, radiations, energy movements, almost everything, like a global brain—where we will be able to instantly read ocean temperatures, see every form of energy waste, measure the tiniest atmospheric changes, connect every “spring uprising”, enable a Massai warrior, or anyone, to access more information from a smart phone than the Secretary General of the UN could do 10 years ago with an expensive computer.  

 Vince Cerf—one of the founders of the internet itself and now CIE, Chief Internet Evangelist at Google and advocate for good business and society collaborations—helped lead and envision something so bold, mindboggling, and practical that it will become a worldchanging foundation for years and years to come. The Internet we've relied on so far had space for 2^32 addresses—about 4.3 billion. The new, larger IPv6 expands the limit to 2^128 addresses—more than 340 trillion, trillion, trillion!

This step—June 6th 2012—puts the foundation in place for game-changing action. It is no longer utopian to speak about achieving radical energy efficiencies—50% or more. It’s no longer utopian to talk about a Moore’s Law for human cooperation and global action—a doubling of connections every year ad infinity  As the book Abundance writes, “as powerful as the Internet of things will be on our personal lives, it is dwarfed by its business and society potential. Soon companies will be able to perfectly match product demand to raw materials orders, streamlining supply chains and minimizing waste to an extraordinary degree. Efficiency goes through the roof.”  Just the energy savings alone will be world changing. And companies are now teaming up with NASA to put sensors all over the planet to provide real-time information about climate change. Would we have a chance to move from an unsustainable to sustainability + flourishing world in the next 25 years without this?  

I don’t think so.  

That's why Vince Cerf and Google should be nominated for a Nobel-like Prize. From his Google office, on the day Ipv6 launched this past summer Vince Cerf said:

 “Today we launch the 21st century Internet: you ain’t seen nothin’ yet.”
