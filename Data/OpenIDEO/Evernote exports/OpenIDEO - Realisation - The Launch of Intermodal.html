The Launch of Intermodal

June 25, 2012, 11:20PM


On OpenIDEO we celebrate that our community members can join our challenges in whatever way works best for them: from adding content and comments, to reading posts and getting inspired. Nicolas Mika is an example of a community member for whom the Vibrant Cities Challenge planted an inspiring and important seed – one that’s led him to a new initiative he and his group are championing in Grand Rapids, Michigan called Intermodal.

Intermodal is a creative project aimed at transforming a vacant lot in Grand Rapids into a vibrant, urban cultural hub, one that could provide the infrastructure needed to enable entrepreneurs to experiment with new business concepts. Comprised of refurbished shipping containers, Intermodal will house local food producers, artists, or other merchants to showcase their products and connect locally with consumers.

We caught up with Nick to learn more about his efforts and how OpenIDEO inspired this new project:

What motivated you to bring vibrancy to your community in Grand Rapids?

My background is in commercial real estate development so the idea of rethinking and repurposing space for community vibrancy really resonates with me. When I first found the Vibrant Cities Challenge on OpenIDEO, I was inspired to read about spaces like Proxy in San Francisco, and I wondered to myself, why couldn’t we do something like that here? From there my interest in vacant and unused lots just grew! Over time my team at the Urban Renaissance Group (where I work) identified seven urban lots in Grand Rapids that we thought had potential, and we just started brainstorming what we could do with them. The Inspiration and Concepting phases in the Vibrant Cities Challenge were really valuable for us at that point – everyone’s stories and ideas served to open our eyes to what might be possible and inspire our own efforts on the ground.


Intermodal construction underway.

How did you decide on Intermodal? What steps did you take to get started?

It all starts with a good idea. After all, a good idea attracts a lot of supporters and is easier to make happen. Finding that good idea, however, is the challenging part! Each neighborhood, city street and vacant structure in Grand Rapids is different. By taking the the time to understand each place, we were able to design a project that will hopefully have the most impact and ultimately transform the neighborhood.

Next, it’s all about stakeholders; in fact a common theme in all of our brainstorming sessions is creating value in a big way for all the stakeholders of the project. We started out by talking with everybody we could – architects, investors, the planning commission, local community members, and others – to get a sense of what was appealing to them, what they saw as roadblocks to success, and what they needed from us in order to get on board with our efforts. We then proposed various ideas that were inspired by the Vibrant Cities Challenge, ranging from rooftop greenhouses, ZipSpaces, and temporary parks.

We ultimately decided to use refurbished shipping containers to create a pop-up village featuring street food and local products as a way to connect the community together. We’ve since narrowed it down to one potential site in Grand Rapids and we’re aiming to include 8-10 vendors in the beginning, although the goal is to scale this in terms of number of vendors and maybe even the number of locations.

Intermodal is a very ambitious project and could give Grand Rapidians something that doesn’t exist yet: a street food culture. While it’s taken us almost a year of iterations before we felt confident we had a fit, it’s amazing to see it come together and to see people from our community coming out of the woodwork to help support and bring it to life.


The Intermodal pop-up coffee house at TEDxGrandRapids.

What have been your early keys to your success?

As I mentioned, getting the Grand Rapids community stakeholders onboard has been hugely important. Also, being open to prototyping – and potentially failing in the process – has been big for us. In an effort to experiment a bit, we brought the first working prototype of Intermodal – a pop-up coffee house – to this year’s TEDxGrandRapids* event. Not only did it help us build buzz, but we learned a lot about what works and what doesn’t in this kind of space. We’ve also learned that being open to failure is crucial; in the end we know that if this doesn't work on a specific site, we can share with others the story of why it didn't work and offer our learning so others can be successful in the future.

Cheers, Nick, on your terrific efforts bringing vibrancy to your community! We can’t wait to hear how Intermodal develops over time.
