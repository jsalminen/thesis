Realtime Recycling Recs

Ever had trouble finding out what to do with an item you own once you could no longer use it (i.e. a mobile phone that's been replaced, clothing that a child's outgrown, or a television that broke down)? Was the item ultimately reused or recycled?
 
 
Realtime Recycling Recs aims to solve this issue by giving you convenient instaneous recomendations for how to recycle (reuse, revamp, repurpose, rent, resell, etc.) the item in your location at the moment of end-of-use, purchase, and other key moments of impact. To get a RRR (pronounced "ARR!" like a pirate) you could simply scan the green QR code on an item using a phone app, a scanning device available at communal trash/recycling spots such as an apartment disposal room, or an in-store scanner.





Information (address/distance, $, environmental benefit, durability) on local RRR options would then appear like Sharing/Renting (through sites like NeighborGoods.net), Craigslist, upgrading & repair shops/instructions, reuse craft projects, the local Salvation Army, Best Buy electronics recycling dropoffs, or the information that the item can be recycled simply by placing it in the nearest single-stream recycling bin.




Ideally, the QR codes would be visible directly on the item (whether through direct printing or options like labels and hangtags) and the outer packaging. And the regular UPC/barcode could also be RRR scannable. Furthermore, to support basic cellphones (not just smartphones) an item category name could also be printed on the label that could be text-messaged to a # to get a RRR. The distinctive green color of the RRR code will ensure its easily seen by people , as well as incentivizing companies to use RRR codes as a symbol demonstrating their environmental commitment to responsible end-of-use.

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

This safeguards human & environmental health, by making it simple to find easy, responsible ways to "RRR" items you no longer use, electronics or otherwise.



Perhaps even more impactfully, this also allows you to to check the "RRR" options of an item, even before you buy.
 

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

This exposes people to the possibilities for responsible "RRR"ing and makes it easy for them to follow through (through what they choose to purchase, and what they do with an item when they can no longer use it), sheds light on the advantages & disadvantages of different options, and incentivizes manufactures to consider lifecycle from the start to get a green QR code seal of approval.
 

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

Let's make an app for that! For a bare minimum pilot, just need a phone app that scans pre-exiting barcodes and display an integration of the relevant data (Directories of various "RRR" options already exist. This is about making them easily accessible at the right moment.).
 

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

In many cases RRR information can be initially linked to item categories (i.e computer), rather than specific individual products (i.e Macbook Pro), decreasing the amount of information that needs to be gathered. Also, data on various reuse options and locations is often publicly available: http://www.bestbuy.com/site/null/Recycling-Electronics/pcmcat149900050025.c?id=pcmcat149900050025, http://www.salvationarmy.com, http://www.earth911.com, etc. With a reasonable amount of work, these already digitized data sources can probably be integrated into the service.



There will need to be some mechanism to ensure this remains updated, whether through open APIs, Wiki-like functionality that allows anyone to contribute, or a more closed system with participating companies to ensure quality.



And a sustainable revenue model would need to be determined, such as company sponsorships or seal licensing, to fund keeping the network running and recruiting of participants (companies, "RRR" providers, & individuals).
