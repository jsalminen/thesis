REGENERATE! -- The Online Refurbished Electronics Store

Consumers often don't need electronics that are state of the art, and refurbished tvs, cell phones, and computers would be valuable as a cheap alternative for people who don't have the "cabbage" to spring for the latest and greatest tech.
http://www.flickr.com/photos/juniorvelo/4996819836/sizes/l/in/photostream/
The store would refurbish and create valuable electronics from e-waste. People often need something that will function, like a cell phone that they'll use only until their contract is renewed, or a laptop that they can just use for basic functions.

Electronics become updated faster then they break in many cases, and most broken electronics still have many valuable parts.  The online store could create value by taking the broken or out of date electronics and refurbishing them back to functional electronics.  Many companies resell refurbished electronics as a cheaper option to the newest technology, allowing two markets for resell, to the consumers directly via website and to the stores that sold the electronics in the first place.  

A person with e-waste would enter their electronics on the website and list any defects or what is not working and then get an immediate cash quote and shipping labels to ship the e-waste directly to the refurbish center.  At the center if the product has other issues not mentioned then the cash delivered would be less than the quoted amount.  This would be a more realistic deposit program due to the huge variety in consumer electronics.  Then the center would fix the issues or take out the parts that can still be used in other electronics to resell.  

Summary of Benefits:
E-waste that is unusable would be properly disposed of
For usable parts it adds another round of use before heading to the landfill 
Good potential for sustainable profits 
Majority of e-waste collecting could be free with bins or free packing slips online
 

How does your concept safeguard human health and protect our environment?

It allows a second round of use for e-waste instead of scrapping all. It also would allow what waste did make it to the store to be disposed of responsibly.

Where does your concept fit into the lifecycle of electronic devices?

It fits in the post use, but pre trash part of the current cycle of electronics. It creates more of another market, one that isn't commonly practiced yet. The goal would be to get the used electronics market as booming as the used car market.

What steps could be taken today to start implementing your concept?

Acquiring knowledgeable people in the computer, phone, and television fields who would repair and recreate the products out of e-waste. Also warehouse and manufacturing space would be needed to house and repair the parts. A website would need to be set up and then the re-imbursment program could be set up.

What kinds of resources will be needed to fully implement and scale your concept?

Knowledgeable people who are good at making things work and overcoming obstacles. Partnerships with recycling centers and waste management companies would be needed to dispose the unusable e-waste properly. The company could start with just laptops and then move into all computers and then into cell phones and tvs and slowly branch out to cover all e-waste. Money for storage space, initial shipping costs, webspace, salaries, and equipment would be needed too.

Great idea Greg!
Dell and a few other companies are already doing this with ex-rental and bulk contract clients who turn over recent product quickly (while they are still attractive on the secondary market) - see
( http://www.openideo.com/open/e-waste/inspiration/electronics-companies-getting-involved-today/ ).
Setting up something more general makes good sense - this could be a marketplace for locally 'regenerated' items where the regeneration is achieved through a network of 'recognised' refurbishment firms with access to the spare parts database. This is done in the auto industry where parts are catalogued for trading between repair shops. So an PC with a sound motherboard might only need another video card - or alternatively the motherboard might become a component in a refurbished PC elsewhere.
The Philippines has a thriving secondary market for some 'repaired' electronics even to replacing laptop screens.
I have a Dell laptop with an (almost monochrome) red screen - a company in the US has quoted me on supplying replacement components to fix it myself (which I may get around to doing).
