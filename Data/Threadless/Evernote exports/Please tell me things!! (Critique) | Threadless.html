Slowing down is hard but very helpful. A lot of what taz says has worth to it in a brutally honest view of peoples work. Sometimes we need a kick in the shin in order to stop moving forward before we walk off a cliff with a design that is not ready to fly.

A suggestion with illustrator that helps me be more creative: Try making your own brushes and then applying them to the edges of your illustration to give it depth or style. You can do this by making a simple horizontal narrow shape in black then drag that shape into your Brushes Pallet window and select "Art Brush". Experiment and try everything. I once drew some fish and then used them as a custom brush to make my design.

Also start off by posting your idea in sketch form so you don't do too much work on an idea that might need to be changed a lot.

If you aren't willing to change a design, don't ask for feedback. Also, I'm not sure what was insulting about Taz's comments. There was no attack, just a personal assessment.

One that that irks me - and it's not just you that says it, so don't take this as a personal attack - is the "well you don't submit artwork, so how can you judge mine" fallacy.

Ever had a meal you really liked or really didn't like? Are you a professional chef? No? Then how DARE you judge that meal? You can't possibly know what is pleasing to people unless you are a professional.

If anything, someone like Taz's feedback is the MOST important because: a) It isn't sugar coated. She definitely has her own blunt style, but good. Sugar coating helps nothing. It's up to the receiver of feedback to make it "constructive" by doing something with it, not the giver of the feedback. All the giver needs to do is be honest. b) she's a consumer of Threadless. She votes and she buys shirts - THAT'S your target market, not other artists.

As for some specifics on this design, itself, as others said above, the mouth placement is off and the proportions don't quite work. The hand is large as if to indicat it's closer to the viewer but it also appears to be right up against the mouth. Try some foreshortening if you still want the large hand.

Also, the little cereal man with his arms up right by the mouth makes it look as if his arms are actually some sort of smile on the boy's face (since they line up with the corners of his mouth.) That's actually how I initially saw them, so you may want to adjust placement there, too.
