Unofficial Threadless Guide


Welcome to the unofficial Threadless guide! This is a short, four-chapter guide that will help beginners get their footing here while also acting as a resource for the veteran designer to continue honing their skills.


Chapter 3


In this chapter, we'll cover everything there is to know about what goes on between finishing putting the final stroke on your design and finally having it submitted to Threadless.


Preparing Your Submission


The first part of submitting is getting your artwork on the submission file. There are two elements to the submission file: the first is a large preview image. The second is a smaller thumbnail GIF which is used on the blogs and your profile to link to the design. We'll go through these one at a time.


The Preview Image


The preview image needs to show the entire design to the voters and also where it's going to go on the shirt. Both of these need to be in every preview.


You have a choice for the type of preview you want to do. The traditional preview image is just a JPG image, like the one below



alternatively, you could use a nifty Flash presentation like this one.


I tend to stick with the traditional JPG presentation. Mobile devices can't (and won't ever) be able to display Flash, and I like everyone seeing the same presentation. But that's just me.


The next two sections will be creating just a JPG submission and a Flash submission respectively.


JPG Preview


With the JPG preview, we need to both show the details of the design and the placement of the design in one, 640x800 image. I think there are pretty much two ways to go about this. The first is the JPG image like the above, or the one below



With a preview like this, the design is shown largely in the background. Then, in the foreground is a small shirt that shows where the placement is.


Another option is just putting the design on a large shirt, like this:



I'll show you how I go about making a preview like the first one I showed.


First, I create a new document at 150 DPI and 640x800 pixels.



On the design, I right click all of my work (not the background) and duplicate it to the new document. In my case, I always keep my design in a folder called "Final."



After being duplicated, it's the active selection on the new document. Typically, though, it's too big to even see! By pressing Ctrl+T, you'll bring up the transformation options. Adjust the scaling until it fits nicely in the JPG.



Once it's scaled to the right size, I change the color of the background layer to the color of the shirt. I tend to use the alt+delete method we used in chapter 2.



Finally, I'll get an image of a t-shirt to show the placement. There are a few options for this. I prefer to juse use the Threadless tee template pack that I used above. Alternatively, here's a Flickr group where awesome photographers put up blank photos for Threadless designers to use for free. You can view the pool of images here.


Once I have my shirt, I copy just the shadow and tee color layers over to the presentation document. I resize it, and move it toward the bottom of the presentation.



The final step is marking where the design is going. I usually just place text, or an X.



Of course, you can follow these same steps to make the other type of presentation, too. Just make the shirt bigger and put the design there instead.


Flash Preview


Even if you don't know any Flash at all, you can make a Flash presentation for Threadless! This is because some really swell designers have decided to share their Flash templates with you. All you have to do is open up the .FLA file within the Flash program and make two quick file substitutions. It's incredibly easy. And yes, I know, people here are awesome.

GIF Thumbnail


Now that our preview image is ready, we need to make the GIF thumbnail. This is the little icon you see on the forum and on my profile to represent the design. This can be anything; there aren't any rules about it. Some people crop a piece of their design for the preview; others look around on the internet for an image. You can even make it animated, so long as it stays below the 250kb size limit.


For my design, I'm going to Google search 'slippers' and use some image I find there.



Inside Photoshop, I then create another new document with the right dimensions: 100x70 pixels, 150dpi. I open the slippers image inside Photoshop, duplicate the layer into the document I just made, and scale it to be the right size.



That's all there is to it.
