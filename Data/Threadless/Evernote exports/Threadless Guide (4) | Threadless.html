Unofficial Threadless Guide


Welcome to the unofficial Threadless guide! This is a short, four-chapter guide that will help beginners get their footing here while also acting as a resource for the veteran designer to continue honing their skills.


Chapter 4


In the last chapter, we got the presentation of our design together and sent it in. This chapter will tell you everything there is to know about what comes next.


Design Rejected


Your design doesn't go up immediately after you submit it. This is because a member of Threadstaff reviews it first to make sure it's not offensive. Sometimes, however, she might get the sense that it won't do well in scoring. Because of this, it will be rejected and won't go up for scoring.


Every rejected design comes with an e-mail explaining why. Sometimes you'll get a vague e-mail that suggests something along the lines of "making it better." This can be discouraging.


(Note: If you didn't get the e-mail and you use Gmail, check both your spam and junk folders on Gmail. Who knew you had two different folders for email you didn't want?)


Very rarely, designs are rejected that aren't bad at all. If you have a feeling your design should have gone up for scoring, you should ask people here at the forum what they think. If they say "Yeah, that's strange!", then you should contact Threadless about it and they'll put it up for scoring for you. But sometimes you just have to accept it for what it is. If you're new here, you should assume that it wasn't a mistake. Chances are you've yet to get a sense of the type of designs that do well.


My tip is to always keep in mind that Threadless doesn't print all types of art. For instance, a certain degree of complexity (either conceptually or aesthetically) is required for the design to do good. Contemporary art that sells for millions of dollars would never do well on a Threadless shirt. Your art isn't bad if Threadless doesn't like it...it's just not Threadless.


But anyway, if your design is rejected, you'll ultimately need to do one of two things:



    • Seek help from someone here and act on the advice they give.

    • Start a new design.


    The second of those options can seem really extreme, especially after you've put so much work into the design. But you'll get to the point where you don't get too attached to any one design of yours. This is a good thing! This matter will be discussed further in a moment...


    Design Accepted


    Of course, if your design isn't rejected, then it must have been accepted! Usually within a day or two (but not over the weekend) your design will go up for scoring. You'll get another e-mail letting you know this. Yippee!


    Promoting Your Design


    There is no real reason to promote your design excessively. If you want a few of your friends or fans to come vote on it, go for it. But bringing in hundreds of voters is a different story.


    Now, this isn't against the rules, but Threadless already has a bunch of voters here who will see your design regardless. If your art actually is printable, it'll do well whether or not you summon people to vote well on it.


    To further dissuade you, the community notices when large numbers of people come to vote on a single design, and it usually isn't looked upon positively.


    Even more, I believe there to be philosophically sound reasons why bringing too many fans or friends to the competition can mess it up.


    But do what you want. Personally, I'd refrain from calling friends or fans here.


    Finished Early


    At any given time on Threadless, there are hundreds of designs up for scoring. Threadless has a system in place to remove the designs that are doing the worst from scoring. After the first 24 hours, if your design has below a 1.7-or-so scoring average, it will be dropped and you'll be notified.
    Don't be too distraught -- take it as criticism from your client (the community of voters). Keep working and keep submitting.


    skinnyStats


    While your design is scoring, and after you've given it a score, you can see how the score is doing. This is displayed off to the right on the page where you vote for the design.
    Then, after scoring is completely done, you have access to what are called skinnyStats. skinnyStats are displayed in the same place as the scoring average, but you can see a lot more than just that. You can see how many people voted 0, 1, 2...et cetera, how many people said "They'd Buy It," and more.

    Interpreting Your Score


    Here is a quick break-down of what the different scores mean:


    1.7-2.3 Lowest scores If your design is in range, it may appeal to a niche or need some work. Lots of people have designs score in this range -- especially your first few designs (just look at mine). Low chance of getting printed.


    2.3-2.6 Medium scores Your design is probably a step in the right direction but could be made even better, probably! Some designs in this range are printed.


    2.6-3.0 Medium-high scores These are pretty decent scores! Nice work. It wouldn't be surprising if it gets printed, especially if there are lots of "I'd Buy Its."


    3.0-4.0 High score Many of the community's favorite designs score in this range, and also a large number of designs are printed that are in this range.


    4+ Legendary score Only a few designs have ever done this. This usually happens because the design goes viral and gets hundreds of users registering to give it a 5. Here's one of those famous designs:



    Other Factors


    Threadless doesn't just decide what it prints from scores. It uses other factors, too. One good indicator is the number of comments. If your design has just a few comments (less than 20), then you can tell people weren't really feeling it. If your design has 50+ comments, then it was a bigger hit and may have a better chance at getting printed.


    The checkbox "Id Buy It!" seems to also factor in to what gets selected for print. One of my prints 'Four Spirits' had about 50 I'd Buy Its out of about 500 votes. That's a rate of 10%, which I think is pretty good. Some of my designs get very few...one of them got just 3!


    So Many Zeros!11!!1


    A common complaint from new users is the number of zeros you received on your design. Like every other negative thing that's been mentioned in this blog, don't worry about it. Firstly, 'zero' doesn't necessarily mean that they think it has no value, or anything like that. It just means they really didn't want to see your design on a shirt.


    Now, some people are like 'yeah well...I WAS DOWNVOTED!' Downvoting is when people vote zero on a design to increase their own chances of getting printed.


    It's easy to see if you were actually downvoted with a little math. Specifically, with the normal distribution. The normal distribution describes many systems in the universe. When a system is normally distributed, plotting it makes a bell curve.



    Threadless voting has all of the properties of a system that would be normally distributed. If your skinnyStats looks like a bell curve, then you probably were not downvoted, no matter how many zeros you have. Let's look at some skinnyStats:



    80-some zeros. That's a lot right?! Nah, it fits a bell curve. It's fine.

    You can gather more about your design from the shape of the bell curve



    Getting Printed


    Threadless will, in most cases, send you the e-mail saying you got printed within one month of scoring finishing. They never decide not to print your design, though. Two things follow from this: firstly, you'll never get an e-mail saying they won't ever print your design; you'll only get an e-mail saying that you are getting printed. Secondly, they could e-mail you at any time asking to print it: it could get printed a year after you submitted it!


    In the e-mail, they ask you to upload your design at a particular URL. After this, it's a few weeks before your design goes up. I think it was about 2 months between when I got the e-mail and when my design went up for sale. They send you the money for the design near to when the design prints. I think I got mine the week before it went up for sale.

    Not Getting Printed


    There comes a time in many designers' lives when they want to give up on Threadless. They are drawn in with the promise of money, and are let down when their efforts go unrewarded. It's happened to me, and I've heard others mention similar experiences. You shouldn't give up, though.


    Being dissuaded from Threadless comes from two factors. First is taking low scores too seriously. The second is a lack of getting printed. Whatever you do, these things cannot prevent you from continuing to submit. If you get dissuaded from submitting, you'll never get printed! You need to find a way to handle these two facts: the majority of many people's designs do poorly, and the vast majority of your designs won't ever printed.


    For me, my decision not to give up came with a transition about why I stick around Threadless. It no longer became about getting printed. It became about improving as an artist. Over the years, I've improved greatly, and this is what pushes me forward. If I submit a new design I'm proud of, I'll remain pleased with it even if it scores poorly (this is good for me, 'cause most of my subs do poorly). Now I don't worry at all about whether or not I'm going to get printed. I assume I won't, and carry on having fun making submissions regardless.


    Keep it up! It took me 3 years and about 30 designs to get printed.

    Submitting Elsewhere


    Threadless isn't the only t-shirt competition website. There are many others, too. If you don't get printed here, you can try your luck at those other places. Designers tend to wait at least 3 months after scoring finishes before they try submitting their design elsewhere.


    Do note that Threadless is the highest paying of all of the websites. Furthermore, if another website prints your design and Threadless e-mails you saying they want to print it, too, you'll have to decline their offer. This has happened to several people, so just be aware of the risk.

And for anyone interested in the "philosophically sound reasons" against calling large amounts of external voters, I'll try to explain it here. But first, a brief recap on where this comes from:

when you rank any system, you compare it against a value system. For instance, good pizza is pizza that tastes good. It happens that we tend to use a metric of taste for evaluating pizza. Other properties tend to not matter as much as taste. For instance, the property of 'Was the pizza delivered on a bike"? We generally won't say that we had good pizza because it was delivered to us by bike.

Our task is figuring out the properties of competitions that make for good competitions.

I think I might have a place to start on our task, as outlined above. To begin, we need to agree that competitions need some system of judging the contestants. Maybe it's something like scoring points, like in basketball. In other competitions, it's a panel of judges. This list isn't exhaustive, by any means; there may very well be more. But I think we can all agree that Threadless is a panel of judges, who evaluate your design by giving a number value.

Now, the same panel of judges needs to evaluate each competitor in the competition in the same way. If this doesn't happen, bad things happen. In an extreme case, say we're testing two slices of pizza. One panel of judges for one of the slices loves all pizza, while a different panel for the other slice hates pizza with a passion. The results from this competition say nothing about the comparative quality of these two slices.

Threadless is interesting in that we do actually switch out the panel of judges for every design. But, as a whole, the Threadless community does tend to like certain things more than others. Scoring isn't random, for the most part. As evidence for this, older members of Threadless can have a general, accurate a sense of whether or not a design will do well.

Now, the panel of Threadless judges is limited in size. It's just a small number of people. If you start calling friends or fans together to vote on your design that is comparable to this number, you're beginning to replace the panel of judges. The exact same thing that happened with the pizza happens. It makes the competition pointless.

To really hammer this in, if Jackson Pollock came back to life and secretly submitted a design here it would do awful. It isn't what the panel of judges that are already here would score high. Now Pollock has probably one-bajillion trillion followers whom he could call and give his design a 5. They'd love to see his design on a shirt. If they come to Threadless, his design will score a huge number. But this is only because we've displaced the Threadless panel with Pollock's own.

To me, I feel like violence is done to competition when things like this happen. It's not about reasserting that people already support your creations. It's about doing well in this particular competition.

To return to Pollock, he could (and should, I think) just print his designs himself and sell them to his fans. Why even come to Threadless?

Now, the pizza and Pollock examples in this post are obviously extreme cases. I use them to show what the worst possible situation is that could happen with a competition that allows for judge replacement. In most cases (like khallion, I think), it isn't this bad. All I'm just saying system that permits these cases is sup-optimal.
