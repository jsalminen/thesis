When Quirky.com was first unveiled in 2009, the product branding process was fairly straightforward. Community members would submit their ideas for names and taglines, then browse the results to vote on their favorites, producing a body of data that was both easy to use and fun to create. It was a simple platform for a simple time. However, 4 years and 400,000 community members later, things have changed. Hundreds of naming submissions have given way to thousands, and the process of picking favorites became an ever more daunting task. In our never-ending quest for improvement, the Quirky Platform team decided that a scalable solution was in order.

That solution was unveiled today in the form of a brand new naming and tagline platform, designed to make the evaluation process more fun and engaging while gathering even more useful data. The process of submitting a name remains unchanged, but the voting system has been replaced with an addictive “This or That” game. Users are shown a pair of potential names, and are asked to pick the one they like best. After a selection is made, each user is presented with real-time feedback on how their choice is ranked, before progressing to the next pair. Twenty completed pairs will automatically earn you a slice of influence, but you can keep playing as long as you can click a mouse. Once the overall phase is complete, the Quirky Design Staff will sift through the highest-ranked submissions to find the name that best fits the product’s design aesthetic. The higher a submission is ranked, the more time we will take in evaluating it.

Now, a shiny new platform is all well and good, but how much does this actually change? According to the data: everything. Under the old system, the task of picking three names out of a pool of thousands became a grueling ordeal, and it showed. Over 90% of name submissions received less than five votes, and over 50% received less than two. This limited pool of popular names made it difficult for Quirky to harness community data when making a final decision. In contrast, the new system ranks names from best to worst under the Elo rating system (used in chess, and made popular by The Social Network). This ensures more even exposure for submissions, and helps surface more popular names for the Quirky staff to choose from. As demonstrated by the graph above, the difference is remarkable.

Ultimately, today’s update is only the first step in our overhaul of names and taglines, so if you have feedback on the new platform or process, please post it in the comments below. All feedback will be passed to the Platform team for review, so this is no time to be shy. We’d love to hear what you think!

  • Juho Salminen

    If I understood the explanation of rating system correctly, the algorithm Quirky uses already addresses many of the problems people have pointed out. Each pairwise evaluation is like a "match" between ideas, where the winner takes points from the loser. The number of points taken depends on the current rankings of the ideas. If a lowly ranked idea (for example a new one, which has not been voted for by anyone yet) wins a highly ranked idea, it will get a huge number of points from a single match and thus rise a lot in the ranking. If both ideas are bad and have low ranks, the winner will get ahead of the loser, but will not rise near the top. A bit like in tennis, if you beat Federer you could be world-famous after a single match, but beating 100 nobodies will not get you to Wimbledon.
