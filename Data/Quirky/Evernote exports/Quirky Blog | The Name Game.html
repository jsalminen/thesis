The Name Game

posted about 3 years ago by jess

What’s in a name?

Well, lots.

After much discussion among community members and within the team, we’ve decided to take a second look at the way we approach similar name and tagline submissions.

We came to a few conclusions…

The following name/tagline variations are more-or-less “identical”:

Name Name (spaces)

NameName (no spaces)

Names (plural)

Name-Name (hyphens)

NameName. (non-disruptive punctuation)

The NameName (articles before/in the name)

Your Name Name (possessive pronouns, etc.)

The following name/tagline variations contain elements that make them “unique”:

Naim Naym (different spellings)(Note: this refers to any instance in which the characters used to spell a word are changed)

Unname Naming (prefixes and suffixes)

Named Name (changes in tense)

In cases of “identical” submissions, we’ll award full influence to the community member that submitted the name/tagline first, even if we end up going with a variation of that name/tagline. So if Joe submits the name “QuirkTown” at 10 a.m. and Sally submits the name “Quirk Town” at 11 a.m., and we select “Quirk Town” as the winning name, Joe would win the round. Even more reason to check all previous entries before submitting your name/tagline!

This clarified way of approaching names and taglines will go into effect for Product 46 tagline and Product 47 name. Confused? We’re happy to answer your questions and continue the discussion on the forum.

  • Clinton

    @Jim, the “Name Duplication Prevention” function: The quirky website doesn’t allow someone to make a submission if the subject line is exactly the same as a previous submission’s.

    (When looking for “exactly the same”, the website looks at the same characters Twitter counts – letters, numbers, spaces, and punctuation count, things like bolding and capitalization don’t.)

    Since we normally put our name submissions in the subject line, that website function could automatically keep us from submitting a name identical to an earlier submission. But only if the QS’s definition of “identical” is the same as the website’s.

    Since the QS’s definition of “identical” is “more or less the same” while the website’s definition is “exactly identical”, that function doesn’t help us.

    For example, if someone submits “Home Town”, the website will not allow someone else to submit “Home Town” (or even “home town”) but it will allow “HomeTown” – the website considers those to be different submissions.

    But since the QS considers “Home Town” and “HomeTown” to be the same, (even though the website does not), the website’s automatic “Name Duplication Preventer” isn’t helpful.

    The result is we all have to search through all the submissions before we submit a name to make sure it isn’t a duplicate,
    We all have to search through all the names before voting to make sure we don’t vote for a later duplicate,
    And the QS has to search through all the names for duplicates before choosing a winner.

    It could be so easy. If the QS’s definition of “identical” matched that of the website we would no longer have to search all the names.

    Tah dah –
    If the website let’s you submit it, it’s unique.
    See one you like, vote for it – simply the fact that you can see it means it’s not a duplicate.
    (But the QS’s definition of “identical” must match the website’s).

    Here’s what’s even worse about the current system: it rewards people for what they “could have done” over those who actually “did it”.
    For example, if I submit “Home Town” and you later submit “HomeTown”, then who should win if the QS chooses “HomeTown”?
    Even though I could have, but chose not to submit “HomeTown”; and even though you actually did submit it; under the current system I win instead of you.
    I know the two are close…nearly identical. But if I submit the one I prefer and it doesn’t turn out to be the one the QS prefers, why should I still get the win over someone who did submit the one the QS prefers?
    I don’t think rewarding “could have done” over “did” is right.

    Here’s the second worse thing about the current system: it limits the selection.
    Since the QS considers “Good and Bad”, “Good ‘n Bad”, “Good & Bad”, “The Good and Bad”, and “Good&Bad” to be identical submissions, when we see “Good and Bad”, none of us are going to submit the others (unless by accident).
    So, the community and the QS only sees one “Good and Bad” variation. i.e., That limits the selection we see.
    Wouldn’t it be better for the community and the QS to see and choose from all the variations rather than have to look at “Good and Bad” then pause to imagine “Good & Bad”, “Good ‘n Bad”, “The Good and Bad”, and “Good&Bad” before making a selection?
    The current system requires that pause-and-imagine of us and the QS. Since few of us do that, we’re not considering many of the variations…and perhaps missing what might have been the perfect name.

    I realize “Good and Bad”, “Good ‘n Bad”, “Good & Bad”, “The Good and Bad”, and “Good&Bad” are very close.
    I know that the QS considers it more humane to give the submitter of “Good and Bad” the win if “Good&Bad” is chosen. But I disagree. If I submit “Good and Bad”, it was the one I chose from among the variations (yes, I believe submitters consider all the variations before submitting the one they consider to be the best). I don’t believe it’s inhumane to hold submitters to their judgment and choice.

    I also realize that there is some concern over sneaky copiers submitting a variation of a good name.
    But, two things to consider: First, who is a “sneaky copier”? I think someone who submits what they think is a better variation of a name they like is the kind of collaborator quirky wants in the community. But, in my book, a “sneaky copier” is someone who submits a name they don’t like simply because they think it might win for some reason. And how often are sneaky copiers going to win with a name they don’t like? How often can they tell which name is going to win? I’d guess never.
    So, why do we deny ourselves the convenience of the website’s automatic “Duplicate Name Preventer” because of concern over something that’s not ever going to happen anyway?
    Second, as I point out above, I believe submitters consider all variations before choosing one for submission. When a submitter chooses one variation over another, they have, in effect, discarded the other. Whether the submitter of the other is a sneaky copier or a collaborator, they are more like recyclers who have actually served quirky by placing the discarded name back before the community for consideration…and, so, do deserve the win if the their submission is chosen.

    Since the benefits are so huge, and the problems are so small, I hope the QS will make their definition of “identical” match the website’s. Then the automatic “Duplicate name Preventer” can make the name phase a lot easier for us all.
