Invention Picks of the Week!

posted about 19 hours ago by baron

Last night’s Eval was not an everyday occasion. Between a panel appearance by Google guru Jeff Jarvis, and a visit from inventor Steve Balassone, our regular Thursday broadcast became quite a star-studded affair. However, all the glitz and glamour didn’t distract from our ultimate goal: over the course of an hour, 14 of the week’s best inventions were discussed and debated, with five ultimately being chosen for Quirky’s collaborative development process. If you missed the party, a full recording of the event can be viewed at the end of this post, but for those on a schedule, here’s a quick run-through of our newest Quirky inventors:

The first invention of the night came from Omid Rad, who pitched a specialized weed killer that directly injects a small dose of herbicide, allowing gardeners to target infiltrators while leaving friendly plants in peace. Omid’s third-generation prototype showed that the concept had legs, and while some viewers expressed concerns about the use of herbicide, the potential of Omid’s idea made it too good to pass up. Ultimately, we decided to select his concept as an explore, and have asked Omid to send us his prototype for some good old fashioned R&D. Between you and us, we don’t doubt that it works just as well as he claims! (Video Segment)

The second pick was submitted by inventor Dale Eickhoff, who showed off his hardware chops with a simple but effective combination chisel, which features multiple chisel heads that fold and unfold like a pocketknife. With 33 years of construction experience, Dale’s expertise was unquestionable, and when other hardware experts voiced their support, citing its versatility and the benefit of concealing the blades, our decision to move forward was a no-brainer. It shouldn’t be long before this handy helper joins RotoBlade and Trimline in Quirky’s growing hardware portfolio. (Video Segment)

In a notable turn from functional to funky, the next winning invention was a device dubbed the TubbleFaucet, invented by Justin Medeiros. A father of two, Justin was tired of wasting water every time his kids wanted to play with running water in the bath, so he devised a tool that would pump bathwater out its own spigot, allowing kids to play to their hearts’ content without running up the water bill. His initial concept met with a bit of skepticism, especially since electric pumps and water rarely get along, but an alternate hand-pumped version addressed that issue quite nicely, and received enough hands for a winning vote. With vast potential for other fun features and attachements, this is one Community Design Phase you won’t want to miss. (Video Segment)

The next winning idea also came from a parent: a horizontal storage cubby invented by Lisah Hartke, which hangs conveniently in the unused closet space beneath your coats and shirts. Despite its  function, the concept received a nearly unanimous vote, as it embodied all the traits that a home-based invention should have, being simple, unique and most of all, effective. If this doesn’t make a splash at Target and Bed Bath & Beyond, we don’t know what will. (Video Segment)

The last invention pick of the night was a bit of an outlier, pushing the boundaries in all the right places. Long-time inventor Amy Pickens submitted an idea for a high-tech haptic compass, to be worn on the wrist, which would tell users which way they are pointing by feel alone, without the need for visuals. Amy proposed that it could be used to give wearers an innate sense of direction, but the Eval crowd was quick to think of alternative applications, such as sightless orienteering for cyclists, drivers and the blind, along with an app-enabled version that would lead you towards a given location in Google Maps. Regardless of how the final product turns out, we have no doubt that it will turn heads. (Video Segment)
