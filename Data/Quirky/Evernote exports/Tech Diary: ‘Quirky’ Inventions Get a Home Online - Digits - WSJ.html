By Andy Jordan

A New York start-up is hoping it can use the wisdom of the crowd to help spur inventions.

The company, Quirky, bills itself a social product-development company, bringing together a community to invent solutions to common problems.

Here’s how it works: Someone in the online Quirky community submits an idea for a product. The community votes on it. Quirky selects two ideas a week to take through design, branding, and manufacturing and put them to market.

“We make invention accessible,” says Quirky’s 24-year-old founder, Ben Kaufman, repeating the company motto like a mantra.

“Our job is to act as sort of shepherds of our inventions. People will submit ideas to the site in various forms. Some are fully formed concepts with renderings and beautiful schematics,” says Kaufman. Some are problem statements: ‘Wouldn’t it be cool if … doesn’t it suck that?’”

The inventor of each product gets a cut of the sales. Quirky gives 30% of its direct sales revenue and 10% of indirect sales to the people who come up with, vote on and help refine the product. The inventor takes the lion’s share of that cut.

One of the first stars in the two-year-old company is 22-year-old Jake Zien, a Quirky community member who has created the Pivot Power, a bendable power strip that allows users to plug in multiple bulky power plugs at the same time. Zien has already made about $30,000 on the Pivot Power, which has been on the market for just a month. For every $30 direct sale, Zien makes $3.39.

“I never… ever dreamed that it would be … in stores around the country, particularly with my face on it,” he says.

Zien, as the inventor, gets his picture on the back of the Pivot Power packaging, like all inventors of Quirky products. He shares that space with the “influencers,” people in the Quirky community who help refine the product, with ideas on color, branding, product design, and function. There are about 72,000 influencers on Quirky, and there were 709 on the Pivot Power.

All 709 of those influencers get a cut of the revenue as well, based on an algorithm Quirky’s founder Kaufman and cohorts developed that quantifies someone’s online influence. The proprietary formula is based dozens of variables, and so far Quirky has no plans to license that algorithm to other companies.

Kaufman started inventing about seven years ago sitting in a high school math class and came up with an idea on how to conceal his iPod shuffle’s wires so his teacher wouldn’t see. His parents re-mortgaged their house to give his idea a shot. That idea gave birth to an iPod accessory company called Mophie, which he later sold. His ah-ha moment came when he saw his headphone invention on a random woman when riding the subway.

“It was absolutely the best feeling in the world,” he boasts. And he set about trying to give other people that inventor buzz.

“Invention is just sort of engrained in us as human beings. If you look at kids playing, they’re inventing,” says Kaufman. “And for some reason, just society or whatever it is just scrapes it all away from you, and makes you feel like you can’t do it.”

Not all ideas make it through the Quirky process. As the founder likes to say: Killing brands is as important as creating them. Failure is part of the plan for success.

Quirky’s founder says the community has to like the idea… and it has to solve a problem. And then Quirky brings in its team of experts to figure out if they can make a valid business case for it, manufacture it and make money doing so.

Zien says he was initially apprehensive about sharing his idea online, but learned that to make it happen, he needed to do just that.

“I think in order to make ideas happen you have to share them with a lot of people, and not keep them to yourself because the first person you tell is gonna steal it,” he says. “I think if you don’t have the energy to go make it yourself, then it’s highly unlikely that person that you ask if it’s a good idea is going to go do it, too.”

He also says he had to learn to take criticism. “… so that when people tear them down, and people can say this could be better and that could be better, it’s not a personal attack … you have to agree.”

Quirky is growing. It just secured $21 million in new funding and is moving to a new 30,000 square foot space in Chelsea. It’s launching two products a week, and has around 25 products shipping.

Sundance Channel has created a reality-TV show based off of Quirky.

“It really chronicles not just the stories of our inventors but the stories of how those products became real products,” Kaufman says. “We see the need of the inventor but then we see, alright let’s put it through industrial design and engineering and prototyping and manufacturing and all the different things it takes to bring a product out into the real world.”

“There’s never been a show on television that really shows the real world how to make stuff,” Kaufman says. The series launches August 30 at 10 p.m. on Sundance Channel.
