Invention Picks of the Week!

posted 16 days ago by Invention Ambassadors

Another week has flown by at QHQ.  This week’s exciting Eval spotlighted 13 idea submissions.  The staff ended up crowning three new inventors and one explore.  Here is the breakdown of last night’s picks:

Our first inventor HSingh has been a member of Quirky for over a year.  His idea for the Adjustable Tip Pen was inspired by his daughter who loves to design handmade cards and invitations.  He noticed her using multiple pens of the same color when working with card stock and wondered why.  She explained that she needed different thicknesses for various designs and script.  That was his “A Ha” moment.  Wouldn’t it be more convenient to just use one pen and adjust the thickness with the turn of a dial?  The staff certainly thought it would be.  With the help of our community the product team will soon be diving into the best way to tackle this project. Our second pick of the night marked the crowning of Quirky’s youngest inventor ever.  Jason Diaz submitted the idea for All in One Toothbrush on behalf of his son, five year old Houston. When Houston tries to put toothpaste on his brush most of it ends up squirting into the sink.  With the help of dad, he built a brush head that fits onto standard toothpaste tubes. Now he is a pro at brushing and wanted to share his invention with the world. When he was notified earlier that afternoon that their idea would be presented at Eval, he and Jason decided to come see the show for themselves (it turns out they live here in NYC).  Houston’s pitch (the tie helped) won over the Quirky staff.  We are looking forward to working on this project. (video segment)

Tiia Nightingale is relatively new to Quirky, having joined us about six weeks ago. She found herself frustrated with traditional methods for adding waves or curls to her hair. Irons can fry your hair and D.I.Y. methods of braiding or twisting wet hair to create waves take too long.   Her idea for a Heated Scrunchie sparked quite the debate amongst the staffers. Her proposal to create a scrunchie filled with poly crystals that would be microwavable in order to produce the same effects as curlers met with quite a bit of skepticism.  There were some doubters in the audience about the feasibility of this product.  However a few believers made the point that if this really worked it would revolutionize the hair care market and that we should at the very least test this idea.  That is exactly what the staff decided to do when they chose it as an explore. (video segment).

For our last pick of the evening, inventor Seung-jae Kim pondered over why people had to purchase both a laptop case and a separate stand for propping it.  His solution, was for Handled MacBook Case that would double as a stand.  The handle would have a flexible piece that would allow it to be manipulated into a fulcrum that could prop the computer when needed. When you are done working you could simply fold it back down, grab the handle and go.  This submission had an incredible amount of community support, with 448 votes.  The staff obviously liked it too because Seung-jae Kim is now a first time Quirky inventor.

Those are all the picks for this week.  If you missed out on Eval this week you can catch all the action on the full video below.  Have a fantastic weekend of creating and inventing.  We’ll see you back here on Monday.

Toothbrush Combined with Floss/Toothpaste Why We Like It: In general, the consolidation of two products into one is something to be praised, especially if the two separate functions are somehow related. Like the ubiquitous Spork, these dental products seek to combine two associated tools —either a toothbrush and floss, or a toothbrush and a toothpaste tube— to make a common activity more convenient. Could there really be a downside? Why We Haven’t Picked It: Unfortunately, there are several. While the consolidation of products is admirable in theory, it’s only worthwhile if you’re actually make a task more efficient. Since dental products are generally stored in the same place (the bathroom), it isn’t difficult to keep these products together, and both applying toothpaste and flossing are relatively simple to perform. Combining a toothbrush with floss or a toothpaste tube may streamline the process a little bit, but the added functionality can lead to any number of complications: a built-in floss dispenser would be difficult to refill, while a toothpaste-dispensing toothbrush would be a nightmare to clean. Combine this with the over-saturation of the dental hygiene marketplace, and the differing life spans for various consumable products (a toothbrush may last five months, while floss may run out in one), and you end up with a solution that causes more trouble than its worth.
