What We Can Learn from Crazy Japanese Inventions

posted 5 days ago by baron

In 1995, Japanese inventor Kenji Kawakami coined the term Chindōgu (literally, “unusual tool”), to describe the practice of inventing oddball solutions to everyday problems. These inventions are often described as “unuseless”, as they solve legitimate household problems, but do so in such an awkward and embarrassing way that few consumers would actually use them. Chindōgu inventions come in a variety of shapes and sizes, but most involve the combination of two distinct products, such as a hat and umbrella that blend to form a hands-free umbrella. More examples can be found in Kawakami’s book, 101 Unuseless Japanese Inventions, and the internet has no shortage of Chindōgu material either.

So, how can these outrageously funny products apply to a practical inventor? The trick is to realize that many Chindōgu inventions, silly as they are, are only a few steps away from genius. Combining two completely different products may seem ludicrous at first, but it forces you to reframe your chosen problem, and to address it from an entirely new angle. It also forces you to think imaginatively, which is an essential component of robust innovation.

Quirky’s own Bobble Brush is a good example of this practice in action. While a cup and bobble toy may have nothing in common, the two products were combined to form a new and useful tool that keeps your toothbrush upright no matter what. Stem is another practical tool born of an unlikely fusion: a straw and spritzer may not cross paths very often, but in this case, the two joined forces to form a truly unique and innovative invention.

Thus, the next time you sit down to brainstorm an invention, start by examining current product solutions, and make a list of all the ways that they can be improved. Then, make a list of separate products that provide these improvements in a different context (i.e., if you want a toothbrush holder that’s impossible to knock over, list a bobble toy as inspiration). Once this is complete, try combining products from those two lists in crazy and creative ways, refining the combinations to make them as practical and efficient as possible. The results might very well surprise you.
