Prototype Like a Champ With MAKE: Projects

posted about 1 month ago by baron

We’ve said it before, and we’ll said it again: nothing sells an invention concept better than a bona fide prototype. Whether it’s crafted from brushed aluminum or rough-cut plywood, a prototype brings an otherwise imaginary concept into the physical world, quashing viability concerns, and helping viewers envision your design as a real, purchasable product. However, while many inventions can be crafted from surprisingly simple components, other prototypes demand a higher degree of complexity, and may seem well beyond the abilities of a novice inventor. After all, you can’t prototype an app-enabled toaster with paper and tape.

That being said, if you’re an inventor who wasn’t born with hammer in hand, you shouldn’t give up hope. The maker movement has inspired a whole new generation of educational resources, one of the foremost of which is MAKE: Projects, a DIY project library curated by MAKE Magazine. The site features a wide variety of DIY concepts conceived by MAKE staff and readers, ranging from simple 2-hour crafting projects to full-blown, month-long masterpieces. If your dream is to build your own robotic butler, MAKE will show you how.

While robo-hacking your life may be pay-off enough, MAKE: Projects can also serve inventors as a makeshift prototyping boot camp. Most projects place a strong emphasis on education, and scale by difficulty, allowing readers to progress from novice to master over the course of several ventures. Whether you’re seeking an intro to electronics, or a 101 on new materials, you’re guaranteed to find a project (or ten) that’s right up your alley. To start, we recommend browsing their general projects page, but here is a selection of project categories that may strike a chord:

Computers and Mobile: While Arduino and the Raspberry Pi both have their own project pages, C&M offers a comprehensive intro into the world of electronic tinkering. If you’re developing a digital invention, and have never touched a circuit board in your life, this is your square one.

Energy: While we’ve never concealed our doubts regarding solar products, alternative energy is here to stay, and if you’re going to develop a solar-powered concept, you might as well do it right.

Hacks: This category is more diverse than most, as it involves any project that modifies a household object. This makes it an ideal category for prototyping, as it teaches you to incorporate existing products into your design.

Robotics: Let’s face it, if you don’t have “build a robot” at the top of your bucket list, you need to reconsider your priorities. This category may be ambitious, but if you’re looking to build a prototype that truly astounds and amazes, this is your port of call.
