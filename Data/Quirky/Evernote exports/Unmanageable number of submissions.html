Unmanageable number of submissions

Number of submissions is getting unmanageable lately - this week especially. I think when number of submissions is over 50 or so, none of the ideas get a good feedback/rating/voting. People are just trying to get through it to earn influence.

I am pretty sure this has been suggested before, but I think as Quirky is getting bigger and more people submit their ideas here, there has to be a different way to rate/vote for the ideas. Here is what I suggest:

Have all ideas go through 2 rounds.

Round 1: Each person gets randomly assigned 50 ideas that s/he rates/votes/comments on.

Top 50 from Round 1 + as many Wild Cards as Quirky staff selects make it to Round 2. Those people that voted for those ideas that made it to Round 2 earn influence.

Round 2: Everyone is free to vote/rate/comment and earns influence for voting on winning idea - basically the same as it is now but with a manageable amount of ideas - only 50+!

I would love to see this change. What do you guys think?

    In my opinion, the solution to this isn't things like limiting the number of submissions, adding hurdles (such as raising the price), or requiring a certain level of effort or expertise in the submission, etc.

    The solution lies in changing the evaluation system.

    Since there are two characteristics of the current system that make the number of submissions an issue, these seem like the obvious places to start

    (Characteristics:)
    1. The system assumes we are making our selections from all the submissions. The fact that we actually aren't (because it's impossible) makes the results meaningless to both the inventors looking for feedback and Quirky's community curation concept.
    (Analogy: If you ask me to taste-test three brands of cola and I take a sip of the first one, then declare it the best without tasting the other two...how meaningful is my opinion to you?)

    2. The community is rewarded based on the outcome of the selection. So, we are very motivated to see every good idea (that pays us), and hate seeing the bad ideas (because they don't...wasting our time).

    (Changes:)
    1. The statistics concept is "...select from every idea presented..." - so, change the number of ideas presented...from "all" to "one (over and over)".

    2. Disconnect the rewarding of % from the outcome of the selection. Connect it to the quality of the voter's evaluation.
    Instead of motivating people to use their votes for guessing what the staff will pick, motivate them to thoughtfully evaluate ideas, whether good or bad.

    When we no longer care whether we see all the good ideas or not, and the Quirky community curation system isn't based on every voter seeing every idea, then the number of submissions will no longer matter (and neither will their "quality").

    Nope, it's not working.

    The core idea behind community curation at Quirky: The "world" can do a better job of predicting which products the "world" will buy than traditional methods.

    But, because of technical problems with the system, the results of the vote are skewed and Quirky doesn't know what the "world" is predicting; forcing Quirky to revert back to traditional methods.

    One of the technical problems: The system assumes that voters choose from among all the submissions presented to them.
    A vote is a "yes", and the lack of a vote is a "no"
    While we know that many times a vote isn't cast because the voter didn't actually see the idea - the system doesn't know that.
    So, the fix is to change how many submissions are presented to voters.
    Instead of presenting 3000+ at one time, present 1 at a time.

    Another technical problem: Human nature.
    People will vote for the submission that is likely to earn them money - whether they like it or not.
    People will vote for their friend's submission - whether they like it or not.
    People will vote for a submission just to join a crowd - whether they like it or not.
    People will vote for a submission that they think Quirky should make - whether they like it or not.
    Which means Quirky is collecting tons of data it doesn't want, mixing it together with the data it does want (did we like the idea), and ending up with a gray mass of unknown.
    The fix is to not collect the unwanted data.
    Control the voting system very tightly, eliminating all the sources of unwanted information.

    Another technical problem: Skimming submissions.
    People want to earn money by voting for all the good ideas.
    So they want to see all the good ideas.
    But, to do that, many end up skimming the submissions - and, in effect, casting their vote more for presentation than product idea.
    Which means it's very possible inventions the world would buy are being missed out on by Quirky, while those the world wouldn't are getting votes like it would - but for presentation.
    The fix is "positive reinforcement" - reward behavior that Quirky wants.
    Instead rewarding those who manage to skim through the ideas as quickly as possible, reward thoughtful input.

    More issues the three tweaks solve:
    Collaboration and interaction are too limited by the requirements of a system trying to also collect meaningful data...
    Inventors are feeling ignored, buried, and lost...
    Many low-level comments are being left, but very little interaction is going on...
    The staff is currently required to curate ideas...
    Evaluating is no fun, and people don't do what is not fun for very long...
    etc...

    Done well, Quirky's community curation system could prove to be so reliable at picking successful products (it really could turn out that the world does a better job of predicting what the world will buy than experts, focus groups, and computer models, etc.) that the staff could step back to a role of simply verifying the viability of the community's choices.

    @Alice, I think that would be an improvement.

    One of the huge design errors of the current Quirky evaluation system (QES) is that it combines a "discreet choice" system (comparing, choosing, and voting from among a list of ideas) with the "show what's bubbling up" system of Digg.

    The Digg system is based on people simply liking something in isolation - there's no "I like this better than that" comparison implied in a Digg.

    While a "discreet choice" system assumes the voters looked at all the ideas presented to them. (In other words, in order for Quirky to trust its assumption that a "vote for A and not for C" means the voter "liked A better than C", a requirement of a "discrete choice" system is that the voter looks at both.)

    That QES design error causes reliability problems with the results, because, at Quirky, voters are currently creating their own subgroups of ideas to look at. Many are looking at A but not at C.
    Mainly by looking at comment count.
    (Not you or I or anybody reading the forums. But the other 90%ish of voters who interact and vote at Quirky only for the chance at influence, and so, do it as quickly and easily as they can.)

    The fix, of course, is for Quirky to assign the sub-groups the voters look at.
    Then, as voters are "looking at all the ideas presented to them", they are looking at a sub-group...and all the ideas are in sub-groups.
    (Then, Quirky can start to make some reasonable assumptions.
    If voter #1 looks at A and B, then doesn't vote for B; and voter #2 looks at B and C, then doesn't vote for C, it becomes reasonable to assume that voter #1 may like A better than C - even though #1 never looked at C.)

    There are a lot of ways for Quirky to control the subgroups.
    They could assign groups of 50.
    Or, there have been suggestions for groups of 10, 2 and 1.

    I prefer giving voters one submission at a time.

    Then, there is no comparison going on. Voters simply dig the idea or they don't.

    And it's easy for voters to look at just one idea. (I think a key to stopping the "short cutting" by voters is for Quirky to tweak the QES until evaluating ideas is the easiest way to have a chance at influence.)

    And I think one round would be enough.
    (Or, this could be the "gains traction" part of the new eval system.)

    [Of course, any of these sub-group ideas requires a big shift in how influence is awarded. If A is the winner, but half the people didn't see A in their sub-group, then awarding influence for voting for A will cause a lot of complaining.

    Since influence is awarded for making an "intelligent contribution", just change how that is defined.

    Instead of defining "intelligent contribution" as "voted for (helped us choose) the winner", define it as "understood the idea in the submission before voting."

    As in, "This voter made an intelligent contribution because they understood the idea behind the submission before voting."

    Then, split up the voting influence among those who put in the effort to understand the ideas.]
