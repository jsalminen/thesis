Invention Picks of the Week!

posted 23 days ago by baron

Happy Friday, makers! It’s been another bustling week at QHQ, but that’s okay, because we’ve got a brand new crop of inventions to develop, courtesy of last night’s live Eval. Out of the 13 product concepts that were discussed, a final four received enough votes to be selected, and will now enter Quirky’s collaborative development process. These are their stories:

The first invention of the night came from Clayton Rogers, a residential architect, who pitched a concept (believe it or not) for a thousand-color pen. His proposed product would contain four base colors —cyan, magenta, yellow and black— which the pen would mix to form any color the user desires. As you can imagine, a number of viewers voiced concerns over viability, but the concept’s incredible potential (especially with a partner app) convinced the Eval crowd that it was worth the plunge. Quirky’s engineering staff will now dive in to figure out how this baby will actually work… we’re betting it won’t be long before they cook up a solution. (Video Segment)

The second pick came from long-time inventor Michael Schecht, who submitted a vent register cover that would give homeowners better control over the HVAC vents scattered throughout their house. As Michael explained: “During the winter the upper floors get too much heat and during the summer the lower floors get too much air conditioning. This means we are constantly tweaking the vent register flaps to try to direct the air into different rooms, and we are heating or cooling rooms when we aren’t using them.” His proposed solution involves an easy-install cover with a thermometer, which would open and close the vent to maintain an even room temperature, regardless of the HVAC’s activity. Once again, concerns about viability were raised, but sheer potential carried the day once again, and Michael’s vent cover will now be joining Boost as his second Quirky invention. (Video Segment)

Next up was inventor Sale Park, who submitted a novel concept for a woodwind reed attachment, made to be used with smartphones. This piece would replicate the physics of a traditional woodwind reed, and convert the sound to digital form, allowing users to play or practice any woodwind instrument directly from their smartphones. We loved this concept not only for its educational value, but for its boundless potential as a digital music device: “This could be a great way to get kids into playing instruments at a very young age,” said Chief Revenue Officer Bill Lee, “plus, there’s so much you can do once the sound file goes into the device.” Well put Bill, well put. (Video Segment)

Diverging from the world of complex electronics, last night’s final invention pick came from Mark De Francis, who invented a clever palm-sized portable grill shaped like a coiled spring, which allows users to adjust its size as the coiled spring expands and contracts. Over the course of the discussion, several portable grills were found to already exist, but none were as distinctive as Mark’s concept, and with the added benefit of adjustable grill size, we felt that this invention was distinctive enough to make a splash, even in a crowded market. That appeal will only grow if our community and designers can make it even simpler, and even more compact, so keep your eyes peeled for a Community Design Phase sometime in the future! (Video Segment)

That, denizens of the interweb, was another week of innovation and invention at Quirky. If you missed out on last night’s festivities, you can watch the full recording below, but otherwise, we’ll catch you next Monday!
