CoolCat | 04/12/2013 | 01:18 PM

When I arrived two years ago, members where screaming for a change because the ideas with the most votes went into eval. Many believed the most popular members had an advantage. So Q changed it to the three+/- highest voted made eval, but the staff entered an additional two wildcard ideas. There were only about 300 ideas submitted a week back then.
Members continued to complain and Q switched over to the present system. Q introduced the Most Active, probably hoping with the huge increase in memberships at that time, it would dilute the most popular members somewhat and help showcase some of the others. In many cases, Most Active creates momentum that often snowballs due to a few variables. Then they pick up votes from the members with little time to look at the massive amounts of ideas, who resort to voting only from the Most Active list. This type of voting also snowballs as the memberships increase.
The final result is very little collaboration between the members and the staff is left with unreliable information. The staff then makes educated and or personal choices at a phenomenal rate to please the ever growing membership base.
The only solution to attain true votes and collaboration that I can think of, would be to divide the ideas amongst the members where each division reviewed 500 ideas or so a week, instead of the current 2,000+/-. Most of the friend voting would be eliminated, as most live in different areas. The top 5 voted on per week from the four divisions, 20 in all, would make UC, and the staff would put in another 20+/-. The staff would select 10 of those 40 to eval. This would give an idea with high votes a better chance to at least make eval and the staff would retain more reliable info.

Clinton Fleenor | 04/20/2013 | 01:05 AM

@RobertFrancois,
Quirky knows "Most Active" corrupts the system*.
But rather than remove it, they came up with this lame attempt to correct that corruption by using an algorithm that will randomly take a very active submission on the first page of "Most Active" and have it "trade places" with a submission with none.

I think they believe the algorithm makes the system "fairer" in some way.
What it really does, of course, - as the activity of the moved very active submission drops off and the moved inactive submission picks up - is confirm how completely "Most Active" corrupts the system.

*And I agree.
Social proof is an extremely powerful form of persuasion. By offering a "Most Active" sort, the Quirky vote is stronger at measuring the effect of social proof than which product ideas we like.
On top of the social proof issue...
(A) Many people want to, or even believe they are supposed to, try to vote for the product ideas they think the staff will pick. They often see "Most Active" is a great predictor of that.
(B) Finally, "Most Active" is easily gamed. Simply line up a dozen friends to both vote and comment as soon as you submit, and you can pop your submission up to the front of the "Most Active" list where the votes and comments start rolling in.

This corrupts the system since "community collaboration and curation" of the product ideas becomes a minority bit of the data the vote is collecting.
