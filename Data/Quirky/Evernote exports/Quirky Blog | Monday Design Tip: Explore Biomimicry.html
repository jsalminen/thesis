Monday Design Tip: Explore Biomimicry

posted 27 days ago by baron

Though we take great pride in our technological achievements as a species, mankind will always be second to the oldest and greatest engineer of all: nature itself. As we struggle to design color-changing materials, tiny flying machines, and underwater vessels, nature has long since perfected all three, and it will be a long time before man-made machines can match the agility of a cheetah, or the intricacy of a mosquito’s wings. However, mankind and nature aren’t always in competition: in fact, some humans are actively working to connect the two worlds, integrating features and technologies from nature into their own product designs. The practice is called biomimicry, and it can yield some truly incredible solutions if you’re willing to keep an open mind.

In most cases, biomimicry refers to a functional feature that was inspired by an animal’s physiology or behavior. For example, the minimalist hose attachment Cobra (above) was inspired by the collapsing fangs of its namesake animal. Similarly, the flexible form of Pivot Power is based on the skeleton of a snake, and the product Hive was inspired by honeycombs, a natural storage solution. Some products even repurpose a natural feature to solve a new problem: a crocodile’s teeth may be made for biting, but the design concept Cocodrillo (below) harnesses their intricate form for storage and organization.

Biomimicry can also take on an aesthetic value, helping to craft a more compelling experience for the end user. While the egg separator Pluck could have taken a variety of shapes, we designed it to resemble an egg: a clever homage to its partner food that makes the product even more fun to use. Similarly, the kid-safe box cutter Box Bug (below) was designed to resemble a whimsical little insect. This not only played off its core functionality (it crawls along cardboard like a bug, and the discarded cardboard resembles an antenna), but the image of a harmless bug plays right into the product’s design ethos, being both safe and child friendly. The result is a product design that intrinsically states: “I am safe, I am creative, let’s have fun together”.

In the end, while biomimicry is a fun way to add some imagination to your design process, it’s important to keep your ultimate goals in mind when exploring the concept. After all, a natural solution isn’t guaranteed to be a superior one. However, if you find yourself in a rut, or if you’re struggling to solve a persistent problem, don’t be afraid to turn on the Animal Channel for a little inspiration. You’d be amazed at how many organic features can be turned into a product (if you think of any other Quirky products that practice biomimicry, post them in the comments below).
