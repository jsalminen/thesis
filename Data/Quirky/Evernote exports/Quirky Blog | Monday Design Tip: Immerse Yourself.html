Monday Design Tip: Immerse Yourself

posted 20 days ago by baron

In his new book, Hidden in Plain Sight, legendary design researcher Jan Chipchase discusses his own intensive process for cultural research, going beyond simple concepts like problems and solutions to delve into the lifestyles of his target demographic, and recognize their unmet needs. It’s a daunting task, even for an expert, but his approach demonstrates the vast value of immersion, which can apply to any designer, professional or otherwise.

As Executive Creative Director of Global Insights for frog, Chipchase analyzes how technology effects people, and in most cases, people who are completely foreign to him. To grasp such an expansive concept, he forgoes traditional surveys and focus groups, believing that the key to lasting impact is a comprehensive understanding of lifestyle, and the way that culture views the world. To this end, Chapchase spends an almost inordinate amount of time on grassroots research: waking up with a community, sampling its food, and chatting with locals as they queue for morning coffee.

“One of my favorite ways to do this early on in the study, and one of simplest, is to wake up with the city,” said Chipchase. “Gather the team before dawn, find an appropriate neighborhood, then cruise around together while the shopkeepers are lifting their shutters, as newspapers hit the pavement and locals step out for their morning constitutionals. The morning rush for essentials–from coffee or chai to fresh pastries to rice porridge–is a ritual virtually everywhere, making it ripe for cross-cultural comparisons. If there’s a long line, all the better: our job, after all, is to strike up conversations.”

Now, Chipchase has made it his life’s work to conduct ethnographic research, so following in his globetrotting footsteps may be beyond the reach of the everyday inventor. However, that doesn’t mean that his system can’t be implemented on a smaller, more moderate scale. The next time you start developing an invention, try blocking off some time to dive into the lifestyle of your target consumer. If you’re designing a product for rice lovers, try making rice with dinner every day for a week, and note any problems you encounter along the way. If you’re designing a product for fitness nuts, try squeezing in a workout each morning. A truly compelling product isn’t designed to solve a single problem, but to fit into someone’s lifestyle in an impactful way.
