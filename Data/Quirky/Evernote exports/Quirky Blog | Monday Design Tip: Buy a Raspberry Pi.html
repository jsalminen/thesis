Monday Design Tip: Buy a Raspberry Pi

posted 13 days ago by baron

If the Wink: Instantly Connected product line wasn’t evidence enough, programming and product design are growing ever more intertwined. While analog products are certainly sticking around, digital connectivity has become a core part of the 21st-century product ecosystem, and for the everyday inventor, the ability to understand and play in that space represents a powerful advantage.

Now at this point, you’re probably thinking: “this all sounds well and good, but I can’t figure out my microwave, nevermind a circuit board.” That’s where the Raspberry Pi comes in. The Pi is a $25, credit-card-sized computer, which was developed to make both electronics and computer programming more accessible to beginners. With only a single circuit board, and a barebones list of components, the Pi is about as simple as a computer gets, making it the perfect foundation for DIY tinkering, both expert and amateur. This means that a creative user can transform the Pi into just about anything, from a video game system or media server to an app-enabled cat feeder. If digital products were multi-colored pictures, consider the Pi a coloring book.

So how can this simple circuit board make you a better inventor? Well, for one thing, it provides a useful intro to computer science, and any designer can benefit from a basic understanding of programming. While you may not have to do any coding yourself, you might have to collaborate with people who do, and having a grasp of their work will make the relationship a lot more productive. However, on top of this very practical benefit, exploring the Pi’s capabilities will give you a better sense of what a web-enabled product can do, and how it can fit into a user’s everyday lifestyle and product suite. In time, you might be able to use the Pi as a base for some incredible prototypes (like this projected speedometer for bikes), but even the most basic familiarity will help you come up with smarter, more creative product concepts.

If any of this sounds intriguing (as well it should), the good news is that is it couldn’t be easier to start. The Pi comes in two models, a simpler Model A for $25, and a more complex Model B for $35 (both available on Adafruit), so a starter kit will fit into even the most frugal inventor’s R&D budget. There are also an abundance of free forums and resources to walk you through the learning process: the Raspberry Pi forum is a great place to start, but MAKE Magazine’s website features a large library of Pi-enabled concepts as well, including a handy start-up guide. If you know of any other helpful Pi-centric resources, or if you’ve already begun tinkering, post a link to your findings in the comments: we’d love to hear about your experience!
