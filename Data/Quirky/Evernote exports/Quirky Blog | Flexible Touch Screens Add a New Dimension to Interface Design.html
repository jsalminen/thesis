Flexible Touch Screens Add a New Dimension to Interface Design

posted 26 days ago by baron

As digital interface tech has developed, the gap between 2D and 3D has grown more and more pronounced. Touch screens have cornered the market on 2D interaction, but most 3D interfaces take a very different approach, emphasizing hands-on manipulation and the use of empty space. However, a team at MIT’s Media Lab is trying to bridge that gap with a “2.5 D” interface called Obake, an elastic touchpad that allows users to push and pull an image, enabling real-time, 3-dimensional input and feedback. In short, think: “moldable touchscreen”.

At first, the concept may seem like something out of science fiction, but the technology behind it is surprisingly down-to-earth.  First, an image is projected onto a flexible surface. Then, as users poke and prod the image, a depth camera records and measures that movement, translating it into digital inputs. At the same time, a set of linear actuators allow the surface to protrude or retract on its own, creating a pushable interface that can also push back.

The first demo video for Obake shows a user interacting with a multi-layered topographical map, but the dev team envisions a variety of applications as the technology grows more advanced. “Let’s take an example—say the toaster,” says Dhairya Dand, one of Obake’s engineers. “Instead of having buttons on the toaster which divorces interaction from functionality, what if, say, you alter the toaster with a malleable surface to make room for more breads of different sizes. You then squeeze the toaster a bit with your hands, the toaster slowly regains its original shape while toasting the bread, and finally the toast pops out.”

Such a complex interface may not be possible just yet, but Dand’s example challenges us to rethink the way we interact with our technology. As 2.5D technology develops, how would YOU like to use it?
