It took a village - or in Internet terms, an online community - to make one special  bag.

San Francisco neuroscientist Darya Pino runs Summertomato.com, a website that offers tips on healthful eating. She goes most weeks to the Ferry Plaza Farmers Market, carrying the same beat-up canvas or cotton bags that most of her fellow shoppers bring  along.

Despite careful packing, there's invariably some food that's hurt in transit, and the high price of farmers' market produce only adds to the damage. "Some stuff gets crushed." she said. "The tomatoes and pears do  worst."

Enter Ben Kaufman. He's the ebullient 24-year-old founder of Quirky.com, an innovative website that brings together those interested in household design - both amateurs and pros - to conceive, design, manufacture and market new  products.

When he met Pino at the Tahoe Tech Talk in 2010, he asked her if she encountered any niggling problems in her day-to-day life. She told him about her beat-up food, and he put the call out to the online community that has gathered under the auspices of his site: Build a better farmers' market  bag.

Peter Wachtel, an industrial design graduate of Brooklyn's Pratt Institute and now a toy designer in Southern California, was one of many Quirky .com visitors who answered the call - and his winning idea addressed far more than just Pino's initial  concern.

The bag he conceived has compartments woven into the side for coddling those fragile pears and tomatoes, but there's also a slot for a wallet and another for an ice pack to keep perishables  cool.

"I also wanted to make it expandable, like my daughter's laundry hamper, so it could adjust to how much you bought," says  Wachtel.

Quirky's New York staff of professional designers took the idea and Wachtel's drawings and honed the plans  further.

Another member of the online community, a marketing professional from Long Island, came up with the name: Mercado - Spanish for  market.

Starting  production

Once the site received a sufficient number of preorders for the bag to ensure that they wouldn't lose money on it, they asked a factory in China to start production. It will be available to the public (in the $30 range) this  month.

"We have some sophisticated designers like Peter who contribute to the site," Kaufman says on the phone from Quirky's New York  office.

"Their ideas come in with great drawings. But we have a whole range of people - grandparents, university students, housewives, teachers - people who just have an idea for something they'd like to see done a bit  better.

"And they send in that idea, sometimes with a rough sketch, sometimes not even that. The mission of this company is to make invention  accessible."

The products aren't ones you'd see in a Museum of Modern Art gift store - not high-end design for design's sake. "We try to solve real problems faced by real people," Kaufman says - and Quirky products are sold at big-box  retailers.

There's Stake, a multipurpose barbecue tool (another Wachtel invention) that will hit the market soon, and a Quirky designer enthuses about a spring they're working on that can attach to a shovel to make throwing snow or shoveling dirt  easier.

Plainly, not every idea is a winner - and sometimes the problems addressed are truly tiny. The little egg mitts (that slip over two fingers and enable picking up a hot boiled egg) or the bowl that helps the cereal stay crunchy might not have impressed Thomas Edison.

The canonical Quirky success story, the one every Quirky user tells you, is of high school student Jake Zien, who came up with an idea for a new kind of outlet strip - one with sections that would move, so it could form a circle and other shapes to fit different spaces and accommodate bulky  plugs.

'I just sent it  in ...'


"I was going to let it drop, with the cost of getting a patent, the difficulty of figuring out a manufacturer. It was too much, but then I just sent it in to  Quirky."

The product, Pivot Power, has been a huge success, selling more than 200,000 since its release in May and earning Zien a six-figure payout to date, according to one Quirky insider. (The company tends to take about two-thirds and the "inventor-ideators" one-third.)

But Quirky doesn't just reward inventors and put their photos on the packages. It encourages community members to participate in many aspects of product creation, from the design, to naming and coming up with a tagline for a piece ("Protect Your Produce" is the Mercado  slogan).

Each person who touches a product in a major way, in Quirky-speak an "influencer," is given a small share of the  profits.

It's a model Kaufman has tried before. When he was in high school, he came to the 2006 Macworld Expo in San Francisco and had a booth where he asked people to doodle ideas for accessories to go with their portable Mac  products.

He posted the better doodles online, got conference attendees to vote on their favorites, then made the accessories, which did well. This enabled him to start Quirky after he dropped out of  college.

Final  say

Quirky, too, relies on its users voting on ideas submitted by random people, but the firm's designers have the final say at each phase of the  process.

"People say we're crowd-sourcing," Kaufman says. "But it's not true. ... We pair the community and experts and give them both a seat at the  table."

The model seems to be working thus far, with its staff growing from four at its founding in 2009 to 80 now, and revenue increasing from $1 million in 2010 to $7 million last  year.

It's in expansion mode, with 35 Quirky products on the market now and another 55 soon to ship  out.

The Quirky community has its discontents - people who grumble that their ideas are never chosen, that percentages are too small, that maybe someone in the vast Internet crowd will steal their concept while it incubates in  public.

With both Mercado and Stake coming out this winter, though, Wachtel is  bullish.

"On other products, I contribute names, taglines, as well as my own ideas. I use lots of different parts of my brain on Quirky - and spend a lot of time doing it. A lot of time. But it's making me a more creative  person."

As for Pino, she's happy with the Mercado bag, and recently took a trip to the Ferry Plaza Farmers Market with a prototype on her shoulder. "It's a new day for my pears and  tomatoes."

Quirky's top innovative products of 2011

Verseur, wine multitool

Created by Angelo Cacchione, London.

All-in-one tool opens, serves and saves wines. Includes a foil cutter, pour spout and stopper for keeping wine fresh.

Price:  $24.95

Solo, collapsible hanger

Created by Howard Tseng, Taiwan.

For people tired of wrestling clothes off hangers: A five-in-one collapsible hanger makes it easy to hang and remove clothes in one motion.

Price:  $10.99

Portion, measuring spoon

Created by Marc Rumaner, Aurora, Ill.

Cooking spoon with integrated measurement tools. The hollow, clear plastic handle doubles as a measuring cup, and a silicone spoon has lines for measuring smaller quantities.

Price:  $8.99

Tether

Created by Gary Rose of Chicago.

Flexible plastic rod stabilizes stemware in the dishwasher. The end clasp clips to wine stems and the hollow base slides over a dishwasher post. Comes in a pack of four.

Price:  $14.99

Broom Groomer, broom-cleaning dustpan

Created by Bill Ward, Ecuador.

A dustpan with rubber teeth that comb out dust bunnies from the broom's bristles. Rubber lip on the front keeps the dustpan flush, built-in foot pedal steadies the dustpan.

Price:  $12
