Steven Shaw | 03/09/2013 | 11:46 AM

That's all very well said.

One thing I'd add is that, while the portfolio may be and continue to be weighted towards simpler products, it's the more complex products that sell for more money. So even though the odds are better of a simple product getting picked -- because our capacity to make those is quite high -- there's a larger potential payoff if you go with something more complex and game-changing. The barrier to entry for the next Pivot Power is higher than for the next cord management item, but the reward is correspondingly higher when you're earning 4.2% perpetual royalty on a $30 or $99 item as opposed to a $3.99 item.

Another thing I'd add is that we are on an upward curve of capacity. We can make things today that we couldn't make six months or a year ago, and in six months or a year we'll be able to make things we couldn't have made today. Every week I see them bring some new piece of equipment into the workshop, and every week there are new designers, engineers, etc., showing up. This is something that's hard to convey, because people understandably look to our current product portfolio to gauge their submissions. But doing that actually puts you behind the curve by a couple of leaps, because what's on sale now was submitted a while ago and what's submitted now won't be on sale for a while.

So yes, I agree, best odds of getting picked tend to be for simpler products. But best potential upside payoff? I'd argue for game changers.
